System Architecture · Built Around the Mission

How the Gideon Generation Platform Works Behind the Scenes

Seven plain-language blueprints showing how a church community funds and supports 300 Oakwood students — from a single monthly pledge to a student's graduation.

How to read these blueprints

Each picture below is a map. Boxes are the parts of the system (people, programs, or storage). Arrows mean "sends to" or "talks to." You don't need to be technical — every map comes with a short explanation in everyday words. The story we keep returning to is the real one your app already tells: Margaret, a church member, sponsors Jordan, an Oakwood freshman, with Rev. Williams as the staff facilitator who keeps everything safe.

📦 = a place that stores things ⚙️ = a program that does work → = sends to / talks to - -> = joins / connects to 🟢 = a low-cost service to use
Design 1 · The Big Picture

The Whole System at a Glance

Think of your website as the front of a building people walk into. Everything in this map is the part they don't see that makes it actually work. A person taps a button on the website or phone app, that tap goes to the gateway (a doorway that checks who you are), and the doorway hands the job to the right service — like a front desk sending you to the correct department. The services remember things in the database, keep big files in file storage, and pass slow chores to background workers so nobody has to sit and wait.

Why it's built this way: keeping each job in its own service means the team can fix or improve one part (say, payments) without breaking the others. The gateway in front is the single guarded entrance, so security only has to be perfect in one place.
Design 2 · Student Funding

How One Monthly Gift Travels to a Student

This is the heart of the whole project: turning a pledge into real support. When Margaret promises $50 a month, the app doesn't touch her card itself — that would be unsafe. Instead it asks Stripe (a payment specialist) to set up an automatic monthly gift. Every month, on its own, the money moves, a receipt is emailed, the gift is applied to Jordan's tuition, and Jordan's "funding progress" bar goes up. Read the loop below top to bottom — the box around the steps means "this repeats every month, automatically."

  Note over App,Pay: The card lives on Stripe — never on our servers

Money tip for a church budget: Stripe (and nonprofit-focused options like Givebutter) have no monthly fee — they take a small slice of each gift. Registered nonprofits can apply for discounted rates, which adds up over hundreds of monthly donors.
Design 3 · Community & Support

The Circle of Support Around Each Student

Funding is only half the mission — the other half is people standing behind a student. This map shows the relationships, and one rule is built right into the design for safety: the donor and the student never talk directly. Every message, letter, and video call passes through a trained staff facilitator (Rev. Williams). This protects the student, protects the donor, and keeps the connection warm and appropriate. The dotted lines show everyone joining the same facilitated video meeting.

Why the facilitator sits in the middle: because this platform connects adults with young people, mediation isn't a nice extra — it's the safety wall. The software should make direct, private contact between donor and student impossible by design.
Design 4 · The Memory

What the System Remembers (and How It Connects)

The database is one big, super-organized set of connected lists. This map shows the important lists and how they link. Read the connectors like sentences: a Conference "has members" who are Donors; a Donor "makes" a Pledge; a Pledge "creates" a Transaction every month. The crow's-foot symbol (the little fork) means "many" — one donor can make many monthly transactions. Notice an Application "becomes" a Student once an admin approves it — that's your pending-apps tab made real.

One quiet but vital rule: always store money as whole cents (5000), never as $50.00 with a decimal point — it prevents rounding mistakes that, across hundreds of gifts, can lose or invent real money.
Design 5 · Cost-Smart Cloud

Who Powers Each Part — Chosen to Keep Costs Low

You don't build most of these hard parts yourself — you rent them from companies that already solved them, picking the ones with generous free starting tiers. This map lines up each need on the left with a low-cost service on the right. The whole right-hand column can start at roughly $5–$25 a month plus the small slice Stripe takes from donations. The riskiest parts (the money, the database, the logins) are handed to specialists on purpose — that's where a cheap shortcut could expose donor money or student data.

The honest bottom line: start near $5–$25/month on free tiers; expect a gradual climb toward $75–$150/month as donors, video minutes, and stored files grow. A fully self-hosted version could be cheaper in dollars — but for a platform holding donation money and young people's information, paying specialists for the risky parts is the responsible choice.
Design 6 · Two Journeys

The Path a Sponsor Takes — and the Path a Student Takes

Architecture isn't only boxes and wires; it's the experience two different people have. On the left is what a giving church member walks through. On the right is what an incoming freshman walks through. Notice the student's second step — "admin verifies enrollment" — that's the part of your app that today is just a checkbox, and it's the one step worth strengthening into real verification before a profile ever goes public.

Design 7 · Build Order

What to Build First (So You're Not Doing It All at Once)

You don't build everything on day one. This map is a recommended order, left to right. Each phase stands on its own and delivers something useful before the next begins. Get the foundation solid, then make the money work, then build the human connection, and only then polish and grow. Following this order means you could quietly launch with real donors after Phase 2, while messaging and video are still being built.

A safe milestone: after Phase 2 you can accept real monthly donations and let sponsors watch funding progress — a genuine, working product — even before the messaging and video features in Phase 3 are finished.
