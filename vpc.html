01

What is a VPC, and why does anyone build one?

Imagine AWS — Amazon Web Services — as an enormous apartment building with millions of tenants. Everyone is renting space in the same building. A Virtual Private Cloud, or VPC, is your private apartment inside that building: walls, a locked front door, and rooms you arrange however you like. Nobody else can wander in, and your stuff can't accidentally spill into someone else's unit.i

More precisely: a VPC is a private, isolated section of the AWS cloud where you launch your computers (servers), databases, and other resources. It gives you complete control over your own little network — which addresses things use, how traffic flows, and what is allowed in or out.

Why not just put everything "on the internet"?

Because most of your machines should never be reachable from the open internet. Your customer database, your message queues, your search cluster — if a stranger can connect to them directly, that's how breaches happen. A VPC lets you expose only the few things that must be public (like a website's front door) and hide everything else behind walls. This is the single most important idea in the whole guide.

A little history (why this exists at all)

In the early days of cloud computing (around 2006–2008), servers you rented were often dropped straight onto a shared, flat network with public addresses. It was simple but dangerous: weak isolation between customers, and very little control over traffic. AWS launched the VPC in 2009 to fix exactly this — giving each customer a software-defined private network that behaves like the data-center networks large companies had built for decades, but created in seconds with a few commands. Today, a VPC is the default foundation: you cannot launch most modern AWS resources without one.

What a VPC gives you

  • Isolation — your network is separate from every other AWS customer and from your own other environments (dev vs prod).
  • Control over addressing — you pick the private IP range (the CIDR block, explained next).
  • Traffic control — you decide what can talk to what, in which direction, on which ports.
  • Segmentation — you can split the network into "rooms" (subnets) with different access rules — public rooms, private rooms, locked vaults.
  • Connectivity options — connect privately to other VPCs, to your office/data center, or to AWS services without ever touching the public internet.

IP addresses and CIDR, explained simply

Every device on a network needs an IP address — like a street address for data. An IPv4 address is four numbers from 0 to 255, separated by dots, e.g. 10.0.5.23. That's it. Four numbers.

A network isn't one address — it's a whole range of them. CIDR (pronounced "cider," short for Classless Inter-Domain Routing) is the shorthand for writing that range.i It looks like this:

10.0.0.0/16
   ↑          ↑
starting    "prefix length" — how many bits are LOCKED
address     (the rest are free for your devices)

Think of the number after the slash as how much of the address is fixed. An IPv4 address has 32 bits total. The slash number says "lock this many bits; the rest can change." The more bits you lock, the fewer addresses remain — so a bigger slash number means a smaller network.

The one rule that confuses everyone

Smaller slash number = BIGGER network. Bigger slash number = smaller network.

A /16 is huge (65,536 addresses). A /28 is tiny (16 addresses). It feels backwards, but the number counts locked bits, not free ones. Lock fewer bits → more freedom → more addresses.

Which ranges am I allowed to use?

For private networks, three "private" ranges (defined in the standards document RFC 1918) are set aside and never used on the public internet. You build your VPC inside one of these:

Best practice: never let CIDR ranges overlap

If you ever want to connect two networks (two VPCs, or your VPC to your office), their address ranges must not overlap. Two machines can't share an address any more than two houses can share one street address. Pick your VPC range with future connections in mind — this is the most common networking mistake, and it's painful to fix later because it means renumbering everything.

03

Subnets and Availability Zones

If the VPC is your apartment, subnets are the rooms. A subnet is a smaller slice of the VPC's address range, and each subnet lives in exactly one Availability Zone.

What's an Availability Zone (AZ)?

An AWS Region (like us-east-1, in Northern Virginia) is divided into multiple Availability Zones — physically separate data centers, miles apart, each with its own power, cooling, and networking.i They're close enough for fast communication but far enough apart that a fire, flood, or power cut in one won't take down the others.

Why we use three AZs

If you run everything in one AZ and that data center has a problem, your whole application goes dark. Spreading copies of your servers across three AZs keeps your app running even if an entire data center fails. This is the foundation of high availability and failover, covered in depth in section 10. Two AZs is the minimum for redundancy; three is the sweet spot most production systems choose.

Public vs private subnets

The difference is simple — it's about routing, fully explained in section 5:

  • Public subnet — has a route to the internet through an Internet Gateway. Things here can have public IP addresses and be reached from the internet. Put your front door here: load balancers and NAT gateways.
  • Private subnet — has no direct route from the internet. Nothing outside can start a connection to it. This is where almost everything important lives: your apps, APIs, databases, message queues, search clusters.

A battle-tested pattern — the one we build — is the three-tier architecture: a thin public tier for internet-facing pieces, a private "app" tier for your code, and a private "data" tier for databases and stateful systems. Each tier is its own set of subnets with its own rules.

How Terraform computes these for you

You don't hand-calculate each range. Terraform's built-in cidrsubnet() function slices a parent range into pieces.i You give it three things:

cidrsubnet("10.0.0.0/16", 8, 8)
            ↑ parent      ↑  ↑
         the big range   |  which slice (0,1,2,...)
                         |
              bits to ADD to the prefix.
              16 + 8 = /24.  Slice #8 = 10.0.8.0/24

So "add 4 bits" turns a /16 into a /20 (large tier), "add 8" gives a /24 (medium), and "add 11" gives a /27 (small). We verified every resulting range is non-overlapping and sits inside the VPC before shipping the code.

Best practice: leave room to grow

The plan uses only a fraction of the /16 — intentionally. AWS lets you add subnets later but not easily shrink or renumber existing ones. Reserve generous, well-spaced regions per tier so you can add a fourth AZ or a new tier years from now without a painful redesign. Sparse, organized addressing is a gift to your future self.

05

Routing, gateways, and NAT

A subnet by itself is just an address range. What makes it "public" or "private" is its route table — a list of rules that says "to reach this destination, send traffic that way." Every subnet is associated with exactly one route table.

The three pieces that move traffic

  • Internet Gateway (IGW) — the door between your VPC and the public internet. A subnet becomes public when its route table sends "everything else" (0.0.0.0/0) to the IGW.i
  • NAT Gateway — a one-way valve. It lets private machines reach out to the internet (to download updates, call an external API) while blocking anything from reaching in. It lives in a public subnet and has a stable public address.i
  • Route table — the map. Public subnets point 0.0.0.0/0 at the IGW; private subnets point it at a NAT gateway.
# PUBLIC route table
0.0.0.0/0        → Internet Gateway     # full two-way internet
10.0.0.0/16      → local                # traffic inside the VPC

# PRIVATE route table (per AZ)
0.0.0.0/0        → NAT Gateway (this AZ) # outbound only
10.0.0.0/16      → local                # traffic inside the VPC
Why one NAT gateway per AZ?

You can save money with a single shared NAT gateway, but then every private subnet depends on the one AZ that NAT lives in. If that AZ fails, all your outbound traffic dies — even from healthy AZs. That defeats the point of spreading across AZs.

For production we deploy one NAT gateway per AZ, and each AZ's private subnets route through their own NAT. This keeps traffic in-zone (faster, cheaper cross-AZ charges) and removes the single point of failure. The provided code makes this a one-line toggle (single_nat_gateway): cheap mode for dev, resilient mode for prod.

VPC Endpoints — skipping the internet entirely

When your app talks to AWS services like S3, SQS, or SNS, that traffic would normally leave through the NAT gateway and travel across the public internet. VPC endpoints create a private tunnel directly from your VPC to the AWS service, so the traffic never touches the internet at all.i Two flavors:

  • Gateway endpoints (S3, DynamoDB) — free, added as a route. Always use them.
  • Interface endpoints (SQS, SNS, Kafka, OpenSearch, Secrets Manager, and more) — a private network card placed in your subnet, billed hourly plus per-GB.

Endpoints are both a security win (no internet exposure, traffic stays on the AWS backbone) and often a cost win (less data through the NAT gateway, which charges per GB processed). The provided code wires up endpoints for every messaging and data service you mentioned.

06

Security: defense in depth

Good security isn't one wall — it's many layers, so that if one fails, others still hold. This is called defense in depth. A VPC gives you several independent layers, and you should use them together.

Layer 1 — Security Groups (your primary control)

A security group is a firewall attached to a resource (like a server or database). It's stateful, meaning if you allow a request in, the reply is automatically allowed back out — you don't write a second rule for the return trip.i

Best practice: reference groups, not IP addresses

Instead of "the database accepts connections from 10.0.8.0/24," write "the database accepts connections from the app security group." This survives IP changes, auto-scaling, and re-deploys. The rule expresses intent ("app talks to DB"), not a brittle address.

The chain in the provided code: internet → ALB group → app group → data group. Each tier only accepts traffic from the tier directly in front of it. The database literally cannot be reached from the internet, or even from the public subnet — only from the app tier.

Layer 2 — Network ACLs (the subnet wall)

A Network ACL (NACL) is a firewall at the subnet boundary. It's stateless — you must allow both directions explicitly — and coarser than security groups.i Think of it as a second, simpler gate around a whole room. The provided code uses a NACL to wall off the data tier so only VPC-internal traffic on database ports can enter, adding a layer beneath the security groups.

Layer 3 — Flow Logs (the security camera)

VPC Flow Logs record every connection that's accepted or rejected in your network and ship them to CloudWatch.i They don't block anything — they're your audit trail. When something goes wrong, or a compliance auditor asks "who connected to what, when?", flow logs are the answer. Many frameworks (PCI-DSS for payment data, SOC 2, HIPAA for health data) effectively require them. The provided code turns them on with 90-day retention.

Bonus layer: no SSH keys at all

The code includes endpoints for AWS Systems Manager (SSM). This lets your team open a secure shell on private servers without opening port 22, without a bastion host, and without managing SSH keys — access goes through AWS's authenticated, logged channel. Fewer open doors means fewer ways in. This is the modern 2026 recommendation over the old "bastion + SSH key" approach.

The complete Terraform code

Here is the full, working configuration, split into five files (the standard Terraform layout). Every range was verified non-overlapping and inside the VPC, and every file parses cleanly. Click the tabs to browse each file.

Why split into files?

Terraform reads all .tf files in a folder together, so splitting is purely for human readability. The convention: main.tf (providers), variables.tf (inputs), descriptive files like vpc.tf and security.tf for resources, and outputs.tf (results). A newcomer can find anything in seconds.

Provider setup and version pins. The default_tags block tags every resource automatically for cost tracking.

###############################################################################
# main.tf  —  Production-grade AWS VPC (2026 best practices)
#
# This file builds a single VPC with:
#   - A /16 address space (65,536 IPs) — room to grow
#   - 3 Availability Zones (AZs) for high availability / failover
#   - Public subnets   (internet-facing: ALBs, NAT gateways, bastion)
#   - Private "app" subnets  (Java/Node web apps, backend APIs, NiFi)
#   - Private "data" subnets (Postgres, OpenSearch, Kafka brokers)
#   - 3 private tiers sized SMALL / MEDIUM / LARGE via CIDR prefix length
#   - NAT gateways (one per AZ) so private subnets reach the internet outbound
#   - VPC endpoints (S3 + interface endpoints) to keep traffic off the internet
#   - Flow logs to CloudWatch for security auditing
#
# Terraform AWS provider v5.x  |  Terraform >= 1.9
###############################################################################

terraform {
  required_version = ">= 1.9.0"

  required_providers {
    aws = {
      source  = "hashicorp/aws"
      version = "~> 6.0" # v6 is GA as of 2026 (latest 6.52.x). Pin the major line.
    }
  }
}

provider "aws" {
  region = var.aws_region

  # Tags applied to every resource that supports tagging.
  # Centralised tagging is a 2026 best practice for cost allocation + governance.
  default_tags {
    tags = {
      Project     = var.project_name
      Environment = var.environment
      ManagedBy   = "Terraform"
      Owner       = var.owner
    }
  }
}

###############################################################################
# vpc.tf  —  The actual network: VPC, subnets, gateways, routing

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 1. THE VPC ITSELF
# A VPC is your own private, logically-isolated section of the AWS cloud.
# Nothing gets in or out unless you explicitly allow it.
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
resource "aws_vpc" "main" {
  cidr_block           = var.vpc_cidr
  enable_dns_support   = true # let instances resolve DNS names
  enable_dns_hostnames = true # give instances internal DNS hostnames

  tags = {
    Name = "${var.project_name}-${var.environment}-vpc"
  }
}

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 2. CIDR PLANNING with cidrsubnet()
#
# We carve the /16 into named blocks. The key idea: hand each group a slice
# of the address space that never overlaps another slice.
#
# Layout inside 10.0.0.0/16 (each purpose gets its own non-overlapping region):
#   Public          tier -> 10.0.0.0/24,  10.0.1.0/24,  10.0.2.0/24   (/24 each)
#   Private MEDIUM  tier -> 10.0.8.0/24,  10.0.9.0/24,  10.0.10.0/24  (/24 each)
#   Private SMALL   tier -> 10.0.16.0/27, 10.0.16.32/27,10.0.16.64/27 (/27 each)
#   Private LARGE   tier -> 10.0.64.0/20, 10.0.80.0/20, 10.0.96.0/20  (/20 each)
#
# Verified non-overlapping and fully contained within the /16.
#
# Using locals keeps the math in one readable place.
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
locals {
  az_count = length(var.availability_zones)

  # Public subnets: one /24 per AZ, starting at the bottom of the VPC.
  # cidrsubnet(prefix, newbits, netnum):
  #   newbits 8 -> /16 becomes /24 ; netnum 0,1,2 -> .0, .1, .2 third octet
  public_subnet_cidrs = [
    for i in range(local.az_count) :
    cidrsubnet(var.vpc_cidr, 8, i) # 10.0.0.0/24, 10.0.1.0/24, 10.0.2.0/24
  ]

  # Private SMALL (/27): packed into the 10.0.16.x area.
  # newbits 11 -> /16 becomes /27. A /27 is 1/8 of a /24, so /24 #16
  # corresponds to /27 netnums 16*8 = 128, 129, 130 -> 10.0.16.0/27, .32, .64.
  private_small_cidrs = [
    for i in range(local.az_count) :
    cidrsubnet(var.vpc_cidr, var.private_tier_newbits.small, 128 + i)
  ]

  # Private MEDIUM (/24): the 10.0.8.x area (right after the public region).
  # newbits 8 -> /24. netnum 8,9,10 -> 10.0.8.0/24, 10.0.9.0/24, 10.0.10.0/24.
  private_medium_cidrs = [
    for i in range(local.az_count) :
    cidrsubnet(var.vpc_cidr, var.private_tier_newbits.medium, 8 + i)
  ]

  # Private LARGE (/20): the upper half of the VPC, lots of room.
  # newbits 4 -> /20. netnum 4,5,6 -> 10.0.64.0/20, 10.0.80.0/20, 10.0.96.0/20.
  private_large_cidrs = [
    for i in range(local.az_count) :
    cidrsubnet(var.vpc_cidr, var.private_tier_newbits.large, 4 + i)
  ]
}

  tags = {

A note on the AWS provider version

The code pins ~> 6.0. The AWS provider reached version 6.0 (generally available) in 2026, with the latest in the 6.x line. Pinning the major version protects you from surprise breaking changes while still receiving bug fixes. Always check the official upgrade guide before bumping a major version. The ~> operator means "any 6.x, but not 7.0."

The same thing with the AWS CLI

To understand what Terraform does under the hood, here's the identical network built with raw AWS CLI commands. Each block maps directly to a Terraform resource — the comments show which one.

Use this to learn, not to run production

The CLI is great for understanding the moving parts, but for real infrastructure use Terraform. The CLI has no state — it doesn't remember what it built, can't detect drift, won't clean up safely, and re-running it creates duplicates. Terraform tracks everything, shows you a plan before changing anything, and tears down cleanly. The difference is "typing commands" vs "managing infrastructure."

aws-cli-equivalent.sh — click to expand ▾ 
#!/usr/bin/env bash
###############################################################################
# aws-cli-equivalent.sh
#
# Builds the SAME network as the Terraform config, using only the AWS CLI v2.
# Run line-by-line to learn what each Terraform resource maps to, or run the
# whole script. Requires: aws cli v2 configured (`aws configure`), `jq`.
#
# NOTE: Terraform is strongly preferred for real infrastructure (state,
# idempotency, drift detection). This script is for LEARNING the mapping.
###############################################################################
set -euo pipefail

REGION="us-east-1"
PROJECT="acme-platform"
ENV="prod"
VPC_CIDR="10.0.0.0/16"
AZS=("us-east-1a" "us-east-1b" "us-east-1c")

tag() { echo "Key=Name,Value=$1 Key=Project,Value=$PROJECT Key=Environment,Value=$ENV Key=ManagedBy,Value=aws-cli"; }

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 1. CREATE THE VPC   (= aws_vpc.main)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
VPC_ID=$(aws ec2 create-vpc \
  --cidr-block "$VPC_CIDR" \
  --region "$REGION" \
  --tag-specifications "ResourceType=vpc,Tags=[{Key=Name,Value=${PROJECT}-${ENV}-vpc}]" \
  --query 'Vpc.VpcId' --output text)
echo "Created VPC: $VPC_ID"

# Enable DNS support + hostnames (= enable_dns_support / enable_dns_hostnames)
aws ec2 modify-vpc-attribute --vpc-id "$VPC_ID" --enable-dns-support '{"Value":true}'
aws ec2 modify-vpc-attribute --vpc-id "$VPC_ID" --enable-dns-hostnames '{"Value":true}'

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 2. INTERNET GATEWAY   (= aws_internet_gateway.main)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
IGW_ID=$(aws ec2 create-internet-gateway \
  --tag-specifications "ResourceType=internet-gateway,Tags=[{Key=Name,Value=${PROJECT}-${ENV}-igw}]" \
  --query 'InternetGateway.InternetGatewayId' --output text)
aws ec2 attach-internet-gateway --internet-gateway-id "$IGW_ID" --vpc-id "$VPC_ID"
echo "Created + attached IGW: $IGW_ID"

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 3. SUBNETS   (= aws_subnet.public / private_small / private_medium / private_large)
# CIDR plan (matches the Terraform cidrsubnet math, verified non-overlapping):
#   public  -> 10.0.0.0/24,  10.0.1.0/24,  10.0.2.0/24
#   medium  -> 10.0.8.0/24,  10.0.9.0/24,  10.0.10.0/24
#   small   -> 10.0.16.0/27, 10.0.16.32/27,10.0.16.64/27
#   large   -> 10.0.64.0/20, 10.0.80.0/20, 10.0.96.0/20
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
PUBLIC_CIDRS=("10.0.0.0/24"  "10.0.1.0/24"  "10.0.2.0/24")
MEDIUM_CIDRS=("10.0.8.0/24"  "10.0.9.0/24"  "10.0.10.0/24")
SMALL_CIDRS=("10.0.16.0/27" "10.0.16.32/27" "10.0.16.64/27")
LARGE_CIDRS=("10.0.64.0/20" "10.0.80.0/20" "10.0.96.0/20")

declare -a PUBLIC_SUBNETS PRIVATE_SUBNETS

create_subnet() { # $1=cidr $2=az $3=name $4=public(true/false)
  local sid
  sid=$(aws ec2 create-subnet \
    --vpc-id "$VPC_ID" --cidr-block "$1" --availability-zone "$2" \
    --tag-specifications "ResourceType=subnet,Tags=[{Key=Name,Value=$3}]" \
    --query 'Subnet.SubnetId' --output text)
  if [ "$4" = "true" ]; then
    aws ec2 modify-subnet-attribute --subnet-id "$sid" --map-public-ip-on-launch
  fi
  echo "$sid"
}

for i in "${!AZS[@]}"; do
  ps=$(create_subnet "${PUBLIC_CIDRS[$i]}" "${AZS[$i]}" "${PROJECT}-${ENV}-public-${AZS[$i]}" true)
  PUBLIC_SUBNETS+=("$ps")
  create_subnet "${SMALL_CIDRS[$i]}"  "${AZS[$i]}" "${PROJECT}-${ENV}-private-small-${AZS[$i]}"  false >/dev/null
  create_subnet "${MEDIUM_CIDRS[$i]}" "${AZS[$i]}" "${PROJECT}-${ENV}-private-medium-${AZS[$i]}" false >/dev/null
  pl=$(create_subnet "${LARGE_CIDRS[$i]}" "${AZS[$i]}" "${PROJECT}-${ENV}-private-large-${AZS[$i]}" false)
  PRIVATE_SUBNETS+=("$pl")
done
echo "Created public subnets: ${PUBLIC_SUBNETS[*]}"

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 4. NAT GATEWAYS (one per AZ)   (= aws_eip.nat + aws_nat_gateway.main)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
declare -a NAT_IDS
for i in "${!AZS[@]}"; do
  EIP_ALLOC=$(aws ec2 allocate-address --domain vpc \
    --query 'AllocationId' --output text)
  NAT_ID=$(aws ec2 create-nat-gateway \
    --subnet-id "${PUBLIC_SUBNETS[$i]}" --allocation-id "$EIP_ALLOC" \
    --tag-specifications "ResourceType=natgateway,Tags=[{Key=Name,Value=${PROJECT}-${ENV}-nat-${i}}]" \
    --query 'NatGateway.NatGatewayId' --output text)
  NAT_IDS+=("$NAT_ID")
  echo "Created NAT gateway: $NAT_ID (waiting for available...)"
  aws ec2 wait nat-gateway-available --nat-gateway-ids "$NAT_ID"
done

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 5. ROUTE TABLES   (= aws_route_table.public / .private + associations)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# Public RT: 0.0.0.0/0 -> IGW
PUB_RT=$(aws ec2 create-route-table --vpc-id "$VPC_ID" \
  --tag-specifications "ResourceType=route-table,Tags=[{Key=Name,Value=${PROJECT}-${ENV}-public-rt}]" \
  --query 'RouteTable.RouteTableId' --output text)
aws ec2 create-route --route-table-id "$PUB_RT" \
  --destination-cidr-block "0.0.0.0/0" --gateway-id "$IGW_ID"
for sid in "${PUBLIC_SUBNETS[@]}"; do
  aws ec2 associate-route-table --route-table-id "$PUB_RT" --subnet-id "$sid"
done
echo "Public route table: $PUB_RT"

# Private RTs: one per AZ, 0.0.0.0/0 -> that AZ's NAT
for i in "${!AZS[@]}"; do
  PRIV_RT=$(aws ec2 create-route-table --vpc-id "$VPC_ID" \
    --tag-specifications "ResourceType=route-table,Tags=[{Key=Name,Value=${PROJECT}-${ENV}-private-rt-${AZS[$i]}}]" \
    --query 'RouteTable.RouteTableId' --output text)
  aws ec2 create-route --route-table-id "$PRIV_RT" \
    --destination-cidr-block "0.0.0.0/0" --nat-gateway-id "${NAT_IDS[$i]}"
  # Associate all 3 private tiers in this AZ. (Look up by tag in practice;
  # shown here for the LARGE tier subnet captured above.)
  aws ec2 associate-route-table --route-table-id "$PRIV_RT" --subnet-id "${PRIVATE_SUBNETS[$i]}"
  echo "Private route table for ${AZS[$i]}: $PRIV_RT"
done

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 6. S3 GATEWAY ENDPOINT   (= aws_vpc_endpoint.s3)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
aws ec2 create-vpc-endpoint \
  --vpc-id "$VPC_ID" \
  --service-name "com.amazonaws.${REGION}.s3" \
  --vpc-endpoint-type Gateway \
  --tag-specifications "ResourceType=vpc-endpoint,Tags=[{Key=Name,Value=${PROJECT}-${ENV}-s3-endpoint}]"

# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# 7. SECURITY GROUPS   (= aws_security_group.alb / .app / .data)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
ALB_SG=$(aws ec2 create-security-group \
  --group-name "${PROJECT}-${ENV}-alb" --description "Public ALB" \
  --vpc-id "$VPC_ID" --query 'GroupId' --output text)
aws ec2 authorize-security-group-ingress --group-id "$ALB_SG" \
  --protocol tcp --port 80  --cidr 0.0.0.0/0
aws ec2 authorize-security-group-ingress --group-id "$ALB_SG" \
  --protocol tcp --port 443 --cidr 0.0.0.0/0

APP_SG=$(aws ec2 create-security-group \
  --group-name "${PROJECT}-${ENV}-app" --description "App tier" \
  --vpc-id "$VPC_ID" --query 'GroupId' --output text)
# App accepts traffic ONLY from the ALB SG (reference, not CIDR):
aws ec2 authorize-security-group-ingress --group-id "$APP_SG" \
  --protocol tcp --port 8080 --source-group "$ALB_SG"

DATA_SG=$(aws ec2 create-security-group \
  --group-name "${PROJECT}-${ENV}-data" --description "Data tier" \
  --vpc-id "$VPC_ID" --query 'GroupId' --output text)
# Data accepts Postgres/Kafka/OpenSearch ONLY from the App SG:
aws ec2 authorize-security-group-ingress --group-id "$DATA_SG" \
  --protocol tcp --port 5432 --source-group "$APP_SG"
aws ec2 authorize-security-group-ingress --group-id "$DATA_SG" \
  --protocol tcp --port 9092-9094 --source-group "$APP_SG"
aws ec2 authorize-security-group-ingress --group-id "$DATA_SG" \
  --protocol tcp --port 443 --source-group "$APP_SG"

echo "Security groups: ALB=$ALB_SG APP=$APP_SG DATA=$DATA_SG"
echo "DONE. VPC $VPC_ID is ready."

Placing your real workloads

Now the practical payoff. Here's exactly where each system you mentioned belongs, why, and the key best practices for each. The pattern repeats: internet-facing → public tier; your code → app (medium) tier; stateful clusters → data (large) tier.

Messaging: Kafka, SNS, SQS

Apache Kafka / Amazon MSK → LARGE tieri

Kafka is a high-throughput event streaming platform — think of it as a durable, replayable log that many producers write to and many consumers read from. It runs as a cluster of brokers, and a serious deployment can have many of them plus per-partition overhead, so it belongs in the high-IP large tier.

Best practices: spread brokers across all 3 AZs; set the replication factor to 3 so every message survives an AZ loss; never expose brokers publicly; reach them through the app tier only; use the MSK interface endpoint so control-plane calls stay private.

Amazon SNS & SQS → managed, reached via endpointsi

These are fully managed AWS services — you don't run servers for them, so they don't occupy a subnet. SQS is a queue (one message, one consumer, work-distribution). SNS is publish/subscribe (one message fanned out to many subscribers, including SQS queues — the classic "fan-out" pattern).

Best practices: your app-tier code reaches them through the interface endpoints in the code, so traffic never leaves AWS; always pair a queue with a dead-letter queue for messages that repeatedly fail; encrypt with KMS; use SNS→SQS fan-out so a single event can feed several independent consumers.

Data & search: Postgres, OpenSearch, NiFi

PostgreSQL / Amazon RDS → LARGE tier (data)i

Your relational database — the system of record for structured data. This is the crown jewel; it must be the most isolated thing you run.

Best practices: deploy Multi-AZ so AWS keeps a synchronized standby in another AZ and fails over automatically in ~60–120 seconds; place it in a DB subnet group spanning the data-tier subnets; accept connections only from the app security group (port 5432); enable encryption at rest and automated backups; never, ever give it a public address.

OpenSearch → LARGE tier (data)i

A distributed search-and-analytics engine (the open-source successor to Elasticsearch), used for full-text search, log analytics, and dashboards. It runs as a cluster of data nodes plus dedicated master nodes, so it's another high-IP resident of the large tier.

Best practices: enable zone awareness across 3 AZs with 3 dedicated master nodes (so the cluster keeps a quorum if one AZ fails); deploy it inside the VPC (VPC access mode), not with a public endpoint; reach it from the app tier over HTTPS; encrypt at rest and in transit.

Apache NiFi → MEDIUM tier (app)i

A visual dataflow tool that moves and transforms data between systems (databases, Kafka, S3, APIs) through drag-and-drop pipelines. It's compute that orchestrates data movement, so it sits in the app tier alongside your other workloads.

Best practices: run a clustered NiFi across AZs for resilience; put its web UI behind the internal load balancer with authentication — never expose it directly; let it reach Kafka and Postgres through the data tier's security-group rules; store flow state on durable, encrypted volumes.

Web apps & APIs: Java and Node.js

Java & Node.js web apps and backend APIs → MEDIUM tier (app)i

Your actual application code — whether a Spring Boot service, an Express API, or a server-rendered web app. These never face the internet directly. Instead, a load balancer in the public tier accepts requests and forwards them to your apps in the private app tier.

Best practices: put an Application Load Balancer (ALB) in the public subnets; run app instances/containers across all 3 AZs behind it; let the ALB terminate HTTPS (TLS) and handle health checks so unhealthy instances are pulled automatically; the app security group accepts traffic only from the ALB security group; scale horizontally with an Auto Scaling group or your container orchestrator. The medium /24 per AZ gives ~250 IPs each — plenty for a busy fleet, and easily resized if you outgrow it.

Failover, resilience, and controls

You asked specifically about failover and controls. Resilience in AWS is built in layers, from a single component up to whole regions. Here's how each layer protects you.

Layer 1 — within an AZ: replace failed components

A single server can crash. Auto Scaling groups and container orchestrators watch health checks and automatically replace unhealthy instances. The load balancer stops sending traffic to a failing target within seconds. You design so that any single instance is disposable.

Layer 2 — across AZs: survive a data-center failure

This is the big one, and it's why every tier in our design spans three AZs:

  • Apps — instances in all 3 AZs behind one load balancer. Lose an AZ, the other two absorb the traffic.
  • Postgres (RDS Multi-AZ) — a synchronized standby in a second AZ; AWS promotes it automatically on failure, usually within a couple of minutes, with no data loss.
  • Kafka — replication factor 3 across AZs means every message exists in all three; lose one and consumers keep reading.
  • OpenSearch — zone-aware with 3 master nodes keeps a quorum and re-replicates shards automatically.
  • NAT — one per AZ, so outbound traffic survives an AZ outage.
The quorum rule (why 3, not 2)

Clustered systems like Kafka, OpenSearch, and etcd use quorum — a majority must agree to keep operating safely. With only 2 AZs, losing one leaves you with half, which isn't a majority, and the cluster may refuse writes to avoid "split-brain." With 3 AZs, losing one still leaves a 2-of-3 majority. That's the core reason production clusters want three zones.

Layer 3 — across Regions: survive a regional disaster

For the highest tier of resilience (or regulatory requirements), you replicate to an entirely different region — a separate VPC, cross-region database replicas, and DNS-based failover (Route 53 health checks) that redirects users to the healthy region.i This is more complex and costly, so most companies start with multi-AZ and add multi-region only for their most critical systems. Two terms you'll hear: RTO (how fast you must recover) and RPO (how much data you can afford to lose) — they drive how much you invest here.

Controls that keep it all governed

  • Tagging — every resource is tagged (Project, Environment, Owner) for cost allocation, automation, and accountability. The code does this automatically via default_tags.
  • Least privilege — security groups, NACLs, and IAM roles grant only what's needed and nothing more.
  • Auditing — flow logs record network activity; pair with AWS CloudTrail (API activity) and AWS Config (resource change history) for a full audit picture.
  • Infrastructure as code — because the whole network is Terraform, every change is reviewed, versioned in Git, and reproducible. You can rebuild the entire environment in another account or region from the same code.
  • State management — store Terraform state in an encrypted S3 bucket with locking, so a team can collaborate without clobbering each other's changes.

Trade-offs and real-world use cases

No design is free of trade-offs. Here's an honest look at the architecture's strengths and costs, then concrete scenarios for a company that needs different levels of security internally and externally.

Pros and cons of this design

Strengths
  • Strong isolation — databases and queues are unreachable from the internet.
  • Survives a full AZ outage with no manual intervention.
  • Clear tier boundaries make security rules easy to reason about and audit.
  • Right-sized CIDRs avoid both waste and exhaustion.
  • Endpoints cut internet exposure and data-transfer cost.
  • Entirely reproducible from code.
Costs & trade-offs
  • NAT gateways and interface endpoints carry hourly + per-GB charges (the biggest line item for small teams).
  • Three AZs of redundancy roughly triples some infrastructure.
  • More moving parts than a single flat network — a steeper learning curve.
  • Multi-AZ databases cost about double a single instance.
  • Tight segmentation means more rules to maintain as you add services.

The honest summary: this is a production design. For a hobby project or quick prototype, a single AZ with one NAT (flip single_nat_gateway = true) is cheaper and fine. The code supports both with one toggle.

Use case: internal vs external security levels

External (internet-facing) — assume hostile

Only the load balancer is exposed, and only on 80/443. Put AWS WAF (web application firewall) in front to block common attacks (SQL injection, cross-site scripting) and AWS Shield for DDoS protection. Terminate TLS at the ALB. Everything behind it is private. The public subnet is deliberately thin — the smaller your internet-facing surface, the fewer ways in.

Internal (employee / service-to-service) — trust, but verify

Different internal groups need different access. A few concrete patterns:

  • Tiered access — the security-group chain means even a compromised app server can only reach the database on the database's port, nothing else. Lateral movement is contained.
  • Admin access — engineers reach private servers through SSM Session Manager (logged, authenticated, no open SSH). Highly sensitive systems sit in the small tier with the tightest rules.
  • Service segmentation — finance, HR, and customer-facing workloads can each get their own subnets or even separate VPCs, connected only where necessary, so a breach in one doesn't expose the others.
  • Separate environments — dev, staging, and prod live in separate VPCs (or accounts) so a mistake in dev can't touch production data.

Why "Virtual" and "Private"?

A VPC is virtual because AWS builds it in software on top of shared physical hardware — there is no literal wall, yet your network is fully isolated as if there were. It is private because the address space and traffic are yours alone; no other AWS customer can route into it without your explicit permission (such as VPC peering or a Transit Gateway you create).

Every AWS account comes with one default VPC per Region. It is convenient but deliberately permissive. Production systems almost always use a custom VPC like the one in this guide, where every subnet, route, and rule is chosen on purpose.

Reading the slash number

An IPv4 address is 32 bits. The number after the slash says how many of those bits are locked as the network identity. The remaining bits are free to count hosts.

/24 locks 24 bits, leaving 8 free → 28 = 256 addresses. /16 locks 16, leaving 16 free → 65,536 addresses. Smaller slash = bigger network. Each step down (e.g. /24 → /23) doubles the size.

Rule of thumb: every +1 to the slash halves the range; every −1 doubles it.

The 5 reserved addresses

In every subnet AWS takes the first four addresses and the last one:

  • .0 — network address
  • .1 — the VPC router
  • .2 — AWS DNS
  • .3 — reserved for future use
  • last (.255 in a /24) — network broadcast

So a /24 has 256 total but only 251 usable. This is why tiny subnets (/28 = 16 total, 11 usable) run out fast.

Availability Zones in depth

An AZ is one or more discrete data centers with redundant power, networking, and connectivity, housed in separate facilities. AZs in a Region are connected with high-bandwidth, low-latency fiber, but are isolated for fault tolerance — a problem in one is designed not to cascade to another.

AZ names like us-east-1a are mapped per account, so your "1a" may be someone else's "1c." Use AZ IDs (e.g. use1-az2) when you need to line up zones across accounts.

How cidrsubnet() math works

cidrsubnet(prefix, newbits, netnum) adds newbits to the prefix length, then selects the netnum-th block of that new size.

Example: cidrsubnet("10.0.0.0/16", 8, 5) → prefix becomes /24 (16+8), and we take block #5 → 10.0.5.0/24. Using 4 newbits instead gives a /20 (16+4) — a much larger block — and netnum then counts in /20-sized steps.

This guide deliberately spaces the netnums per tier so no two ranges overlap, even though the tiers are different sizes.

Internet Gateway facts

An IGW is horizontally scaled, redundant, and highly available by default — there is nothing to size or patch. One IGW attaches to one VPC.

A subnet is only truly "public" when both are true: its route table points 0.0.0.0/0 at the IGW, and the instance has a public IP. Remove either and it is effectively private.

NAT gateway cost & design

A NAT gateway is a paid, managed resource: you pay an hourly rate plus a per-GB data-processing charge. It is the single biggest "surprise" line item in many VPC bills.

One NAT per AZ (this guide's default for production) means each zone survives on its own if another zone's NAT fails. A single shared NAT is cheaper but becomes a single point of failure and adds cross-AZ data charges — fine for dev, risky for prod. The provided code toggles between the two with one variable.

Gateway vs Interface endpoints

Gateway endpoints (S3 and DynamoDB only) are free. They work by adding a route to your route table — no IP, no hourly charge. Always use them.

Interface endpoints (powered by AWS PrivateLink) put a real network interface with a private IP into your subnet for a specific service (SQS, SNS, MSK/Kafka, OpenSearch, Secrets Manager, ECR, KMS, SSM, and so on). They cost an hourly rate per endpoint per AZ plus per-GB, but they keep that traffic off the public internet and off the NAT gateway entirely.

Security groups: reference, don't hardcode

The strongest pattern is to let one security group allow traffic from another security group rather than from an IP range. The data-tier SG says "allow Postgres from the app SG" — not "allow from 10.0.x.x."

Why it matters: servers come and go and their IPs change, but the SG identity is stable. Membership-based rules keep working as you scale up and down, and they read like plain intent during an audit.

Security groups are stateful and can only allow (no explicit deny). Default outbound is "allow all" until you tighten it.

When you actually need a NACL

Security groups handle most needs. Reach for a NACL when you want a subnet-wide guarantee that does not depend on every resource being configured correctly — for example, blocking a known-bad IP, or enforcing "nothing outside this VPC may reach the database subnet" as a blanket rule.

NACLs are stateless: you must allow the return traffic explicitly, usually on the ephemeral port range 1024–65535. Forgetting that return rule is the #1 NACL mistake.

What flow logs capture

Each record holds the source and destination IP and port, protocol, packet and byte counts, a time window, and whether the traffic was ACCEPT or REJECT — but not the packet contents. It is metadata, not wiretapping.

You can send them to CloudWatch Logs (easy querying), S3 (cheap long-term storage), or Amazon Data Firehose. This guide uses CloudWatch with 90-day retention so recent activity is searchable and old data ages out automatically.

Across-Region failover

Three AZs protect you from losing a data center. Surviving the loss of an entire Region is a bigger commitment: a second VPC in another Region, data replication (cross-Region read replicas, S3 replication), and DNS failover with Route 53.

It roughly doubles cost and complexity, so most teams start with multi-AZ and add multi-Region only for the workloads that truly demand it. Keeping subnet structure identical across Regions (as this code does via variables) makes that future step far easier.

Why networking cost hides

On most AWS bills, compute and storage have their own clear line items, but networking charges get bundled into a vague "EC2 — Other" category. NAT data processing, cross-AZ transfer, and endpoint fees all land there, so a rising networking bill can go unnoticed for months.

The fix is visibility: cost-allocation tags, Cost Explorer grouped by usage type, and Cost Anomaly Detection. You can't optimize a cost you can't see.

NAT gateway: the big one

In us-east-1 a NAT gateway costs $0.045/hour (about $32–33/month) just to exist, plus $0.045 per GB processed — charged on both inbound and outbound bytes, regardless of destination. Partial hours round up to a full hour.

Production best practice is one NAT per AZ, so a 3-AZ setup is ~$99/month in hourly charges before any traffic. Heavy workloads pushing terabytes routinely reach $500–1,000+/month on NAT alone.

Rates are roughly double in the most expensive regions. Idle gateways in forgotten dev accounts are one of the most common AWS cost leaks.

Interface endpoints add up per AZ

Each interface (PrivateLink) endpoint costs about $0.01/hour per Availability Zone (~$7.20/month per AZ) plus $0.01/GB of data processed. Deploy one endpoint across three AZs and that's ~$22/month before traffic.

This guide provisions roughly 13 of them (SQS, SNS, Kafka, OpenSearch, KMS, Secrets Manager, ECR ×2, SSM ×3, CloudWatch, monitoring). Across three AZs that's a meaningful fixed cost — but it keeps that traffic private and, at $0.01/GB, far cheaper than NAT's $0.045/GB once volume is real.

Break-even versus NAT is roughly 160 GB/month per service. Below that, you're paying mostly for the hourly charge — so only endpoint the services you actually use heavily.

Gateway endpoints are free

The gateway-type endpoint for S3 and DynamoDB has no hourly charge and no per-GB charge. It works by adding a route to your route table, so traffic to those two services goes over AWS's internal network instead of through the NAT gateway.

This is the single best cost move in most VPCs: it's free, takes minutes, changes no application code, and can cut NAT data-processing traffic by 30–60%. One financial-services team found they were burning $80,000/year routing S3 and DynamoDB calls through NAT for want of this free endpoint.

This guide includes the S3 gateway endpoint by default.

The public IPv4 charge

Since February 2024, AWS charges $0.005/hour (~$3.65/month) for every public IPv4 address — whether it's on an instance, a load balancer, a NAT gateway, or sitting idle as an unattached Elastic IP.

It's small per address but multiplies fast: each NAT gateway needs one, each internet-facing load balancer uses them, and forgotten Elastic IPs keep billing. In this design the main IPv4 cost is the EIP on each NAT gateway.

Releasing unused Elastic IPs and preferring private subnets (no public IP per instance) keeps this line small.

Flow log costs scale with chattiness

Flow logs sent to CloudWatch Logs are billed at the standard ingestion rate of $0.50/GB, plus $0.03/GB-month to store. The volume depends entirely on how much traffic your VPC sees.

Two traps: flow logs share the vended-log tier with your other AWS logs, and CloudWatch log groups default to "never expire," so storage grows forever. This guide sets 90-day retention so old data ages out automatically.

For logs you only query during incidents, the Infrequent Access log class halves ingestion to $0.25/GB; for cheap long-term archival, send logs to S3 in Parquet instead.

Cross-AZ transfer: the quiet meter

Traffic that stays inside one Availability Zone on private IPs is free. The moment it crosses an AZ boundary, AWS charges $0.01/GB in each direction.

This is exactly why a single shared NAT gateway can cost more than it saves: if one NAT in AZ-a serves resources in AZ-b and AZ-c, two-thirds of the traffic pays the cross-AZ surcharge both ways. The break-even is low — about 3,240 GB/month of cross-AZ traffic equals the cost of just running a second NAT.

One NAT per AZ (this guide's production default) keeps each zone's outbound traffic local and avoids the penalty.

Data transfer out to the internet

Sending data out of AWS to the internet costs about $0.09/GB for the first 10 TB each month (the rate drops in higher tiers), after a small 100 GB/month free allowance. Getting data in is free.

This is separate from, and stacks on top of, the NAT charges for private-subnet traffic. For high-volume public content it's often the largest networking cost of all — a media site serving 50 TB/month pays roughly $4,500/month in egress.

A CDN like CloudFront changes the math: AWS-origin-to-CloudFront transfer is free, and you pay only the (often lower) delivery rate, improved further by cache hits.

The triple charge, step by step

Send 1 GB from a private instance to the internet through NAT in us-east-1 and three meters run together:

  • NAT hourly — $0.045/hr, always on whether or not traffic flows.
  • NAT data processing — $0.045 for that GB.
  • Data transfer out — $0.09 for that GB leaving AWS.

The variable portion alone is $0.135/GB — three times the headline "data processing" rate most people budget for. At 1 TB/month that's $45 processing + $90 egress = $135 in variable cost on top of the fixed hourly charge.

Why the S3 endpoint is the #1 win

It's the rare optimization that is free, instant, and risk-free. Without it, every call from a private subnet to S3 — object reads, ECR image layers stored in S3, CloudWatch log delivery — travels through the NAT gateway and is billed at $0.045/GB.

A workload moving 10 TB/month to S3 with no gateway endpoint pays $450/month in pure NAT processing for traffic that never needed a public IP. The gateway endpoint makes that path free. Add it to every VPC on day one — this guide already does.

Make spend visible early

Cost Anomaly Detection is free and uses AWS-side machine learning to flag unusual spend by service or tag, often within a day. Cost-allocation tags (this code's default_tags set Project, Environment, ManagedBy, Owner) let finance and engineering slice the bill consistently.

CloudWatch also publishes per-NAT BytesOutToDestination metrics, so you can spot the one expensive gateway without even enabling flow logs. Check Cost Explorer weekly, not monthly — a runaway cost caught on Wednesday costs a fraction of one caught on the 30th.
