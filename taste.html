Foundation Passage

What Does It Mean to "Taste" God?

The Hebrew word טַעַם (ta'am) — translated "taste" — means more than sampling. It carries the idea of perceiving, discerning, experiencing firsthand. David, who wrote this psalm while fleeing for his life, wasn't offering philosophy. He was testifying from personal encounter. This study explores the biblical thread of personal, transformative experience with God — through Scripture, principle, and story.

Section 1

The Foundation: Psalm 34 in Full

Background

Psalm 34 is an acrostic psalm — each verse begins with a successive letter of the Hebrew alphabet. It was written by David when he feigned madness before Abimelech (Achish, King of Gath) to escape being killed (1 Samuel 21:10–15). From this precarious moment of pretending and survival, David writes one of the most jubilant testimonials in Scripture.

The Invitation in Verse 8

"Taste and see that the Lord is good; blessed is the one who takes refuge in him."

David doesn't say "trust the doctrine about God" or "study theology about God." He says taste. This is an appeal to personal, experiential knowledge — the kind that changes you. The same God who met David in caves, battlefields, shepherd fields, and royal courts wants to meet you personally.

The Three-Part Movement of Psalm 34

1
Praise from Personal Experience (v.1–7): David praises from the overflow of what God did for him — not theory.
2
Invitation for Others (v.8–10): The "taste and see" — he beckons others to come and experience God themselves.
3
Instruction for Living (v.11–22): Practical wisdom flowing from the encounter — this is what a life looks like after you've tasted God.

Key Theological Point

The biblical concept of "knowing God" (Hebrew: יָדַע, yada) is never purely intellectual — it is relational and experiential, the same word used for intimate union. God desires to be known, not merely known about. Psalm 34:8 is the open door to that kind of knowing.

Section 3 — Overview

Personal Encounters: How God Meets People

One of the most profound truths of Scripture is that God never meets two people exactly the same way. He is personal — and He speaks to the person, not a category. Below is an overview of the types of people He encounters and how He connects with each.

Key Observation

God meets people in their actual circumstances — not where they wish they were or should have been. He found Moses at a menial job. He found Elijah in burnout and depression. He found the disciples in their fishing boats. He found Paul on a mission to destroy the church. His meeting place is where you are.

The Leader in Crisis

Moses at the Burning Bush

Exodus 3:1–14
FugitiveHumble WorkOrdinary PlaceCalled by Name

The Context

Moses was 80 years old. Forty years earlier he had been a prince of Egypt with power and privilege — but he had killed a man and fled. Now he tended sheep in the wilderness for his father-in-law. By every measure, his story seemed over. He was ordinary in an ordinary place doing ordinary work.

"Moses was tending the flock of Jethro his father-in-law… and he came to Horeb, the mountain of God. There the angel of the Lord appeared to him in flames of fire from within a bush." — Exodus 3:1–2

How God Met Him

God didn't appear in a temple, a palace, or at a moment of spiritual readiness. He appeared in fire in a bush that didn't burn up — strange enough that Moses turned aside to look. That turning aside mattered. God waited until Moses turned. Then He called Moses by name — twice: "Moses! Moses!" — the double name indicating urgency and intimacy.

The Personal Element

God said: "I have indeed seen the misery of my people… I have heard them crying out… I am concerned about their suffering." (v.7). This is not a distant deity issuing commands. This is a God who sees, hears, and cares — and then says "I am sending you." The encounter was deeply personal: a fugitive shepherd became the deliverer of a nation because God showed up at a bush.

Principle from This Story

God often meets us in the ordinary and overlooked moments of life. The burning bush wasn't spectacular to anyone but Moses — because the encounter was for Moses. He will interrupt your routine to call you by name.

Related: Deuteronomy 34:10 — "Since then, no prophet has risen in Israel like Moses, whom the Lord knew face to face."

The Burned-Out Prophet

Elijah and the Still Small Voice

1 Kings 19:1–18
BurnoutDepressionExhaustionGentleness

The Context

Just days after one of the greatest miracles in Scripture — fire from heaven on Mount Carmel — Elijah collapsed under a juniper tree and prayed to die. "It is enough, Lord… take my life." He was afraid, exhausted, and alone. This is a portrait of clinical depression following spiritual and physical depletion.

"He came to a broom bush, sat down under it and prayed that he might die. 'I have had enough, Lord,' he said. 'Take my life; I am no better than my ancestors.'" — 1 Kings 19:4

How God Met Him

Remarkably, God did not rebuke him. He sent an angel who touched him and said "Get up and eat." Then God fed him, let him sleep, fed him again. Then He sent him on a journey — 40 days to Mount Horeb. At the mountain, wind, earthquake, and fire passed by — but "the Lord was not in" any of them. Then came a "still small voice" (KJV) — a gentle whisper. Elijah came out of the cave.

The Personal Element

God asked twice: "What are you doing here, Elijah?" — not as accusation but as invitation to express the pain. Elijah answered honestly. God met him with gentleness, not drama. Then God reframed his reality: "yet I reserve seven thousand in Israel." You are not alone. This is one of the most tender portrayals of how God meets the broken-hearted in the Bible.

Principle from This Story

God meets the burned-out in gentleness, not more spectacle. Sometimes tasting God is as quiet as a whisper after a long silence. He addresses the body before the spirit — food, rest, then revelation.

The Fugitive Deceiver

Jacob's Ladder at Bethel

Genesis 28:10–22
DreamNight VisionPromiseUnworthy Recipient

The Context

Jacob had just deceived his father Isaac and stolen his brother Esau's blessing. Esau wanted to kill him. Jacob was fleeing with nothing — no merit, no righteousness, no claim on God's favor. He stopped for the night and used a stone for a pillow. That night, God spoke.

"He had a dream in which he saw a stairway resting on the earth, with its top reaching to heaven, and the angels of God were ascending and descending on it. There above it stood the Lord, and he said: 'I am the Lord, the God of your father Abraham…'" — Genesis 28:12–13

How God Met Him

God didn't wait for Jacob to clean up his act. He appeared in a dream — one of Scripture's most vivid — with open communication between heaven and earth. God reaffirmed to Jacob every covenant promise He made to Abraham. Then He added a personal addition: "I am with you and will watch over you wherever you go, and I will bring you back to this land."

The Personal Element

Jacob woke up and said: "Surely the Lord is in this place, and I was not aware of it." Then he was afraid. Encounter with God is often startling — it shatters our assumption that we are beyond reach or beneath notice. Jacob named the place Bethel — "House of God" — because God's presence made an ordinary stone-pillow place holy.

Principle from This Story

God's grace meets us before our transformation, not after. Jacob encountered God at his worst. The tasting of God precedes the change of character — encounter is the beginning, not the reward, of transformation.

The Suffering Outcast

The Woman with the Issue of Blood

Mark 5:25–34 | Luke 8:43–48
HealingFaithDesperationSeen by God

The Context

For 12 years this woman had suffered a hemorrhage. She had spent everything on doctors. She was ceremonially unclean — meaning she was excluded from worship, community, and relationships. She was invisible to society. But she heard about Jesus and decided if she could just touch the hem of His garment, she would be healed.

"She came up behind him in the crowd and touched his cloak, because she thought, 'If I just touch his clothes, I will be healed.' Immediately her bleeding stopped and she felt in her body that she was freed from her suffering." — Mark 5:27–29

How Jesus Met Her

The crowd was pressing on every side — but Jesus stopped and asked "Who touched my clothes?" The disciples were bewildered. But Jesus felt power go out from Him and turned specifically to find this person. He would not let the healing remain anonymous. He refused to let her slink away healed but unacknowledged.

The Personal Element

The woman came forward trembling, fell at His feet, and told Him "the whole truth." Jesus called her "Daughter" — the only time in the Gospels He used this word directly to a person. Then: "your faith has healed you. Go in peace and be freed from your suffering." God refused to let her experience healing without also experiencing being known and named.

Principle from This Story

God is not content with giving you what you need while remaining a stranger. The healing is personal. He will stop the crowd to find you. He wants to look at you, not just pass by you.

The Inexperienced Child

Samuel Hears His Name

1 Samuel 3:1–10
ChildNightAudible VoiceNeeding a Guide

The Context

Samuel was a young boy serving in the tabernacle under the old priest Eli. Scripture notes that "the word of the Lord was rare; there were not many visions" — it was a spiritually dry season. Yet God chose this moment to speak to a child who had no framework for it, no history with God's voice, no training in prophecy.

"The Lord called Samuel. Samuel answered, 'Here I am.' And he ran to Eli and said, 'Here I am; you called me.' But Eli said, 'I did not call; go back and lie down.'" — 1 Samuel 3:4–5

How God Met Him

God called Samuel's name audibly — three times. Samuel kept running to Eli, not knowing it was God. Note that God was patient and persistent — He called again and again. Eli finally realized what was happening and gave Samuel the key instruction: "If he calls you, say, 'Speak, Lord, for your servant is listening.'"

The Personal Element

The fourth time, "the Lord came and stood there, calling as at the other times." The language "stood there" suggests a tangible, physical-like presence. Samuel heard, responded rightly, and God revealed a message. This was the beginning of Samuel's entire prophetic ministry — launched by God calling a sleepy child in the middle of the night.

Principle from This Story

You do not need to be a spiritual expert to experience God. Youth, inexperience, and ignorance are not barriers. He calls beginners. Mentors (like Eli) can help us recognize God's voice — we should surround ourselves with those who can say, "That may be God; listen."

The Disillusioned Disciples

The Road to Emmaus

Luke 24:13–35
DisillusionmentJourneyBurning HeartBroken Bread

The Context

Two disciples were walking away from Jerusalem — the wrong direction from where the others were gathering. They had placed all their hope in Jesus, and now He was dead. Their hopes were "crushed." They had even heard rumors of resurrection, but couldn't believe it. They walked talking about their disappointment.

"As they talked and discussed these things with each other, Jesus himself came up and walked along with them; but they were kept from recognizing him." — Luke 24:15–16

How Jesus Met Them

Jesus joined their walk incognito. He asked questions. He let them pour out their disappointment. Then He began to open the Scriptures — walking them through Moses and all the Prophets, explaining how all of it was pointing to this moment. They were so captivated they asked Him to stay. At dinner, when He broke the bread, "their eyes were opened and they recognized him."

The Personal Element

Looking back, they said: "Were not our hearts burning within us while he talked with us on the road and opened the Scriptures?" They had felt the encounter before they understood it. The "burning heart" is one of Scripture's most beautiful images of experiential encounter — when truth goes past the head and sets the heart on fire. They ran back to Jerusalem immediately.

Principle from This Story

God meets the disillusioned in their journey, not their destination. He walks with you where you are going. The burning heart is a real experience — and Scripture is one of the primary ways it happens. Encounter often comes through the Word opened by the Spirit.

The Religious Persecutor

Paul on the Damascus Road

Acts 9:1–19 | Acts 26:12–18
Blinding LightTotal ReversalEnemy of GodMission Received

The Context

Saul of Tarsus was the most zealous persecutor of the early church. He had overseen the stoning of Stephen. He was traveling to Damascus with official letters authorizing him to arrest Christians and bring them back to Jerusalem for punishment. He was completely convinced he was doing God's will. He was on the way to do more evil.

"As he neared Damascus on his journey, suddenly a light from heaven flashed around him. He fell to the ground and heard a voice say to him, 'Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?'" — Acts 9:3–4

How Jesus Met Him

A blinding light at midday — brighter than the sun. The entire party fell to the ground. But Jesus spoke to Paul specifically: "Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?" The double name again — urgency, intimacy, individuality. When Saul asked "Who are you, Lord?" — the answer was the last thing he expected: "I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting."

The Personal Element

Paul was blinded for three days — eating and drinking nothing. Then God sent a man named Ananias, who addressed him: "Brother Saul." Something fell from his eyes like scales. He was filled with the Spirit, baptized, and ate. His whole life's direction reversed in one encounter. He later wrote that the encounter was so real, he counted all his religious achievements as "rubbish" compared to "the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord" (Phil. 3:8).

Principle from This Story

No one is too far gone for a personal encounter with God. Paul is proof that the fiercest opposition can be converted by a single real encounter with Jesus. God will stop you on a wrong road to redirect you to the right one.

The Forgotten & Abandoned

Hagar: El Roi — The God Who Sees Me

Genesis 16:1–14 | Genesis 21:9–21
AbandonedSlaveSeen by GodFirst to Name God

The Context

Hagar was an Egyptian slave woman — not a Hebrew, not in the covenant family, not in any position of spiritual standing. She had been used as a surrogate by Sarai and then mistreated and cast out. In Genesis 21, she and her son Ishmael were sent into the desert with a skin of water. When the water ran out and she believed her son would die, she set him under a bush and walked away so she wouldn't watch him die.

"And God heard the boy crying, and the angel of God called to Hagar from heaven and said to her, 'What is the matter, Hagar? Do not be afraid; God has heard the boy crying as he lies there.'" — Genesis 21:17

How God Met Her

In the first encounter (Genesis 16), the Angel of the Lord found Hagar in the wilderness and addressed her by name. She was the first person in all of Scripture to receive a direct word from the Angel of the Lord. God gave her a promise for her son and told her to return. In the second encounter, God heard the crying of the child and opened her eyes to see a well of water right in front of her.

The Personal Element

What is most remarkable: Hagar became the first person in all of Scripture to give God a name. She called Him El Roi — "You are the God who sees me." She said: "I have now seen the One who sees me." A foreign slave woman, with no religious credentials, named God from her personal experience of being seen by Him. This is the purest picture of tasting God — unmediated, personal, transformative encounter by the least likely person in the least likely place.

Principle from This Story

God sees the overlooked, the marginalized, the forgotten — those whom no one else notices. Tasting God is not reserved for the spiritual elite. El Roi sees you exactly where you are. Your cry reaches Him even when you believe no one can hear.

Section 4

Types of Encounter with God

Scripture reveals that God connects with people through many different means. He is not limited to one method. His ways are as varied as the people He made.

🌿 The Audible / Visible Word

God spoke audibly to Adam, Moses, Samuel, and Paul. He appeared as fire, light, cloud. Examples: Exodus 3 (Moses), 1 Samuel 3 (Samuel), Acts 9 (Paul). Scripture is now the primary form of the written Word made accessible to all.

Hebrews 1:1-2 — "God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets… in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son."

🌙 Dreams and Visions

Jacob, Joseph, Daniel, Solomon, the Magi — God spoke through dreams. In Acts 2, Peter quotes Joel: "Your sons and daughters will prophesy, your young men will see visions, your old men will dream dreams." This continues in the New Covenant.

Numbers 12:6 — "When there is a prophet among you, I, the Lord, reveal myself to them in visions, I speak to them in dreams."

🤫 The Still, Small Voice

Elijah's encounter shows that God often speaks in a whisper, not in spectacle. The interior sense of the Holy Spirit's leading — a sudden knowing, a persistent impression, a conviction during prayer — this is the most common form of encounter for most believers.

John 10:27 — "My sheep listen to my voice; I know them, and they follow me."

📖 Scripture Opened by the Spirit

Emmaus disciples said "our hearts burned within us." When the Holy Spirit illuminates Scripture, it is not merely information — it becomes a personal encounter. The verse speaks directly to your situation. This is one of the primary ways believers experience God today.

2 Timothy 3:16-17 — "All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness."

🙌 Worship and Prayer

Isaiah saw the Lord "high and lifted up" in the temple during worship (Isaiah 6). The tabernacle/temple was designed as a meeting place between God and people. Deep worship and prayer are environments where many believers report the most tangible encounters with God's presence.

Psalm 22:3 (KJV) — "But thou art holy, O thou that inhabitest the praises of Israel."

🌊 Providence and Circumstance

Jonah experienced God through a storm, a great fish, and a plant. Joseph looked back on years of suffering and said "You meant it for evil; God meant it for good." Sometimes the encounter with God's goodness is only fully perceived in retrospect — when we taste that even the hard seasons were His love.

Romans 8:28 — "And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him."

🫂 Through Other People

Ananias was God's vessel to Paul. Eli guided Samuel. Elizabeth confirmed Mary's calling. The Body of Christ is designed to be a tangible expression of God's presence and love to one another and the world. God often speaks through a mentor, a timely word, an act of unexpected kindness.

1 John 4:12 — "No one has ever seen God; but if we love one another, God lives in us and his love is made complete in us."

Section 5

Personal Application: How to Taste and See

"Draw near to God, and he will draw near to you." — James 4:8

Personal encounter with God is not something we manufacture — but there are postures and practices that position us to receive. Tasting God requires that we actually bring it to our lips.

🔇

1. Create Intentional Silence

God spoke to Elijah in a whisper — not the noise. The crowd, the noise, the screen, and the schedule can drown out the still small voice. Set aside regular uninterrupted time with no agenda but to be with God. Psalm 46:10: "Be still, and know that I am God."

📖

2. Engage Scripture as Encounter

Read the Bible not as a book about God but as God speaking to you. Ask before reading: "Lord, speak to me through this." Lectio Divina — slow, prayerful reading — is a historic practice for cultivating the "burning heart" experience of the Emmaus disciples.

🙏

3. Pursue Honest Prayer

Both David (Psalms) and Elijah ("It is enough, Lord") modeled raw, honest prayer. God does not meet polished presentations — He meets honest hearts. Tell Him the truth about your doubt, pain, and desire. He can handle it; He invites it.

📓

4. Keep a Record of Encounter

Jacob named his encounter: "Bethel" — House of God. Samuel recorded the word given in the night. Write down moments when God seems to speak, a Scripture that hits differently, an impression during prayer. Over time you build a personal testimony that strengthens faith.

🚶

5. Step Toward Him in Obedience

The woman pushed through the crowd. Moses turned aside to see. Samuel said "Speak, Lord." All of them had to make a move. Encounter with God is rarely entirely passive. Partial obedience positions you for deeper revelation. John 7:17: "Anyone who chooses to do the will of God will find out whether my teaching comes from God."

🤝

6. Don't Pursue Encounter Alone

Samuel needed Eli to recognize God's voice. The Emmaus disciples encountered Jesus together. Find spiritual community — a mentor, a small group, a praying friend — who can help you discern and affirm what God is doing in your life. Proverbs 13:20: "Walk with the wise and become wise."

A Prayer to Begin

"Lord, I want to taste and see that You are good — not just read about it. Open my eyes to recognize Your presence. Quiet my heart enough to hear Your voice. Meet me where I am, as You have met others before me. I believe You are good. Help me to experience it. Amen."

Section 6

Reflection & Discussion Questions

Use these questions personally in a journal or as a group discussion guide.

Personal Reflection

Q1

Have you ever had a moment where God felt personally real to you — not just intellectually believed, but actually experienced? Describe it in your journal. What were the circumstances?

Q2

Which of the eight Bible characters in this study do you most identify with right now (Moses, Elijah, Jacob, the Woman, Samuel, Emmaus disciples, Paul, Hagar)? Why?

Q3

What is the biggest barrier to you experiencing God personally? (Distraction? Doubt? Hurt? Busy-ness? Feeling unworthy?) What is one step you could take to lower that barrier this week?

Q4

God met each person in this study in a different way. Which type of encounter (audible word, Scripture, still voice, dream, providential circumstance, through people) do you feel most open to? Why might that be?

Group Discussion

D1

What is the difference between knowing about God and actually knowing God? Can you have one without the other? Is there a danger in having only one?

D2

Hagar — a foreign slave woman — became the first person in Scripture to give God a personal name. What does this say about who God is accessible to? How does this challenge our assumptions about who qualifies for encounter?

D3

The Emmaus disciples said their hearts were "burning within them" while Jesus spoke — but they didn't yet know who He was. What does this suggest about how we might experience God before we fully understand what's happening?

D4

God told Elijah, "There are 7,000 who have not bowed the knee to Baal." Sometimes isolation and exhaustion distort our perception of reality. How do community and honest prayer help us receive a clearer sense of what God is saying?

D5

David wrote Psalm 34 — which contains the invitation to "taste and see" — while pretending to be insane to save his life. What does this teach us about when and where testimony is born? Can you think of something you can now "taste" that once felt like a place of desperation?
