A study in 6 steps · John 10 & Psalm 82

Who is God — and why did Jesus call people "gods"?

A simple, step-by-step look at one of the most twisted verses in the Bible. We'll answer three questions: Who is God? Am I a "god"? And what was Jesus' real point about acting like God? Then we'll see what it has to do with salvation — and how people bend these words to feel superior.

How to use this study Which Bible version?
1

Step one · The setting

The argument that started it all

Imagine the scene. It is winter in Jerusalem. Jesus is walking in the temple, and a crowd of religious leaders surrounds Him. They want a straight answer: are you the Messiah or not? Jesus tells them plainly that He and the Father are united in purpose.

"I and my Father are one." John 10:30 (KJV)

The leaders are furious. They pick up stones to kill Him — and they tell Him exactly why.

"...because that thou, being a man, makest thyself God." John 10:33 (KJV)

So the charge is blasphemy: a human being claiming to be God. To answer them, Jesus does something clever — He quotes their own Scriptures back at them.

What is blasphemy? Why winter in the temple?
2

Step two · Who is God?

First, get this clear: who God actually is

Before we can ask "am I a god?", we have to know what the Bible means by God. The Bible's answer is consistent from beginning to end: there is one God, the Creator, and everything else is something He made.

"In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth." Genesis 1:1 (KJV)
"Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God is one LORD." Deuteronomy 6:4 (KJV)
"...there is none else, there is no God beside me." Isaiah 45:5 (KJV)

So God is the self-existing Creator — no one made Him, He made everyone. He is all-powerful, everywhere at once, and unchanging. Human beings are the opposite: we are created, we depend on Him for our next breath, and we did not exist until He made us. That gap never disappears.

This matters for the whole study. Whatever "ye are gods" turns out to mean in Step 4, it cannot mean we become the Creator — because the Bible has already slammed that door shut. There is only one of those.

3

Step three · The quote

Jesus quotes Psalm 82 — read the whole psalm

Here is Jesus' actual reply to the angry crowd:

"Jesus answered them, Is it not written in your law, I said, Ye are gods? If he called them gods, unto whom the word of God came, and the scripture cannot be broken; Say ye of him, whom the Father hath sanctified, and sent into the world, Thou blasphemest; because I said, I am the Son of God?" John 10:34–36 (KJV)

Jesus is quoting Psalm 82. This is the verse people grab to say "see, we're all gods!" But you can't understand a quote by ripping out four words. You have to read the sentence it came from. So let's read Psalm 82 — it's short, and once you see it, the twist falls apart.

"God standeth in the congregation of the mighty; he judgeth among the gods... How long will ye judge unjustly, and accept the persons of the wicked?... Defend the poor and fatherless... I have said, Ye are gods; and all of you are children of the most High. But ye shall die like men, and fall like one of the princes." Psalm 82:1–7 (KJV, selected)

Notice who is being addressed and what they're being told. They are judges — human leaders who were supposed to rule justly for God — and they are being scolded for being corrupt. The very next breath says "ye shall die like men." That is the opposite of being a real god. Real gods don't die.

Why "gods" (elohim)? Who were these judges? "Scripture cannot be broken"
4

Step four · Am I a "god"?

So… am I a god, or not?

Short answer: no — not in the way the Creator is God. Here is the plain sense of it, step by step:

a.

The word translated "gods" in Psalm 82 is elohim. It usually means God, but it can also be used for those who represent God's authority — like judges acting in His name. It's a title of delegated office, not divine nature.

b.

These "gods" were called that because "the word of God came" to them (John 10:35). They were given a job to do on God's behalf. The title points to their assignment, not to any power of their own.

c.

And the psalm immediately reminds them they will die like ordinary men. So even at their highest, they were still creatures. The Bible is making the gap obvious, not erasing it.

There is a wonderful sense in which believers are lifted up — God calls us His children and even says we share in His nature by grace. But that is adoption into a family, not promotion to Creator.

"Beloved, now are we the sons of God, and it doth not yet appear what we shall be..." 1 John 3:2 (KJV)
"...that by these ye might be partakers of the divine nature..." 2 Peter 1:4 (KJV)

"Partakers of the divine nature" means His character — love, purity, faithfulness — grows in us. It does not mean we become little Creators. A lamp that glows because it's plugged in is not the power plant.

5

Step five · Actions matter

Jesus' real point: act like God, don't just claim it

Here's the part many people miss. In the same chapter, Jesus says don't take my word for it — watch what I do.

"If I do not the works of my Father, believe me not. But if I do, though ye believe not me, believe the works..." John 10:37–38 (KJV)

This connects straight back to Psalm 82. The judges were called "gods," but they were condemned because their actions were unjust — they ignored the poor and protected the wicked. The title meant nothing without the life to match.

Jesus is the reverse: His works prove who He is. He healed, He fed, He forgave, He gave His life. His actions matched His claim perfectly. The lesson for us is simple and serious: being "like God" is shown by how you treat people, not by what you call yourself.

"Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect." Matthew 5:48 (KJV)
"Why call ye me, Lord, Lord, and do not the things which I say?" Luke 6:46 (KJV)

So "being like God" is not a status you announce. It's a way of living — mercy, justice, honesty, love — that you grow into as God works in you.

6

Step six · Salvation & twisted words

What this has to do with salvation — and feeling "superior"

How it connects to salvation. The whole danger in John 10:33 was a human "making himself God." That is actually the original sin — the lie that started it all.

"...ye shall be as gods, knowing good and evil." Genesis 3:5 (KJV)
"For I will ascend into heaven... I will be like the most High." Isaiah 14:13–14 (KJV)

Salvation runs in the opposite direction. We are saved not by climbing up to become God, but by humbly receiving what God did for us. Jesus — who really is God — came down and made Himself low to rescue us.

"...made himself of no reputation, and took upon him the form of a servant... he humbled himself, and became obedient unto death..." Philippians 2:7–8 (KJV)
"For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God: Not of works, lest any man should boast." Ephesians 2:8–9 (KJV)

Notice the last three words: "lest any man should boast." The gospel is built so that no one can feel superior. We're all saved the same way — as a gift.

Twisting words to feel superior. When someone takes "ye are gods" out of Psalm 82 and uses it to claim they are divine or higher than others, they are doing two things the Bible warns against at once:

1.

They wrest (twist) Scripture — pulling a phrase out of its sentence to mean the reverse of what it says. Peter says this is dangerous.

2.

They use it for pride — to feel above others — which is the exact attitude that caused the first fall.

"...they that are unlearned and unstable wrest... the other scriptures, unto their own destruction." 2 Peter 3:16 (KJV)
"Pride goeth before destruction, and an haughty spirit before a fall." Proverbs 16:18 (KJV)

The honest reading of Psalm 82 does the opposite of puffing you up. It humbles you: "you were given great responsibility, you failed, and you will die like everyone else — so live justly while you can." Truth read in context leads to humility; truth twisted leads to pride.

The whole study in five lines

  • →God is the one self-existing Creator. There is no other.
  • →"Ye are gods" was said to human judges who represented God — and who were told they'd die like men.
  • →You're not the Creator, but by grace you can become God's child and share His character.
  • →Actions matter: being like God is proven by how you live, not by a title you claim.
