I

What Is Repentance?

Repentance is far more than feeling sorry. It is a complete turning — of the mind, the heart, and the will — away from sin and toward God.

The Hebrew and Greek Words

נָחַם / שׁוּב

Hebrew — Old Testament

Nacham — to feel deep sorrow, to be moved with grief. Shuv (used 1,050+ times) — "to turn back." A radical about-face. Fundamentally directional: you were going one way; now you go the other.

μετάνοια

Greek — New Testament

Metanoia — from meta (change) + noia (mind). A change of mind that leads to a change of direction. Metameleia — emotional regret only (used of Judas). True repentance is metanoia — not just regret but transformation.

Repentance contains three inseparable elements:

  • 1
    Intellectual recognition — seeing sin as God sees it; agreeing with God's verdict (1 John 1:9 — "confess" = homologeo, "to say the same thing").
  • 2
    Emotional grief — "godly sorrow" over having offended God, not merely fear of consequences (2 Cor 7:10).
  • 3
    Volitional turning — a decisive act of the will to turn from sin and toward God, evidenced by changed behaviour (Acts 26:20).
2 Corinthians 7:10

"For godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret, whereas worldly grief produces death."

Ezekiel 18:30–32

"Repent and turn from all your transgressions, lest iniquity be your ruin. Cast away from you all the transgressions that you have committed, and make yourselves a new heart and a new spirit!"

⚠ God's definition: casting away transgressions AND receiving a new heart — both required.

II

Where Does Repentance Start?

Repentance does not originate in human willpower. Scripture is unanimous: God initiates it — by His kindness, His Spirit, and His grace — before a human heart can respond.

◈ The Theological Starting Point

Repentance starts with God. It is His gift (Acts 11:18), produced by His kindness (Romans 2:4), granted by His Son (Acts 5:31), worked by His Spirit (Zechariah 12:10), preceded by His call (Isaiah 55:6–7). The human act of repenting is the response to a prior divine movement.

The Chain of Initiation

God's Love & Holy Character → He cannot leave sin unaddressed
↓
God's Kindness / Holy Spirit / Preached Word → Creates awareness of sin
↓
Conviction of Sin → The sinner sees their condition
↓
Godly Sorrow → Emotional response to offending God
↓
Turning (Repentance) → Mind, heart, will redirected to God
↓
Faith & Forgiveness → Restoration and new life
Romans 2:4

God's kindness is meant to lead you to repentance — it is the engine that starts the process.

Acts 11:18

"God has granted repentance that leads to life." — Repentance is granted, not manufactured.

Acts 5:31

God exalted Christ "to give repentance to Israel." — It is Christ's gift to bestow.

Zechariah 12:10

"I will pour out a spirit of grace and pleas for mercy" — God pours out what produces mourning over sin.

2 Timothy 2:25

"God may perhaps grant them repentance." — Even opponents need it granted.

John 6:44

"No one can come to me unless the Father draws him." — Drawing precedes turning.

Isaiah 55:6–7

God calls: "Seek the LORD while he may be found." His call precedes our seeking.

Jeremiah 31:18–19

"Restore me that I may be restored." God's discipline begins the turning.

IV

True Repentance vs. False Repentance

Scripture draws a sharp line between genuine repentance and its counterfeits — regret that avoids God, sorrow that merely fears consequences, or reform that leaves the heart unchanged.

2 Corinthians 7:10 — The Defining Verse

"Godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret, whereas worldly grief produces death."

Dimension True Repentance False Repentance
Direction of sorrowGrief over offending God (Psalm 51:4 — "Against you only have I sinned")Grief over consequences, shame, or being caught
Fruit produced"Fruits worthy of repentance" — changed behaviour (Luke 3:8; Acts 26:20)Emotional remorse without lasting change (Hebrews 12:17 — Esau)
Where it leadsTo God, to the cross, to life (2 Cor 7:10)To self, to despair, or to self-improvement (Judas — Matt 27:3–5)
View of sinAs rebellion against a holy God; deeply wrong in oneselfAs a mistake, a weakness, or someone else's fault
ConfessionSpecific, honest, without excuse (Proverbs 28:13)Vague "if I've done anything wrong" — conditional or partial
Response to rebukeReceives correction with humility (2 Sam 12:13)Deflects, rationalises, or blames (Saul — 1 Sam 15:20–24)
Heart changeDesires change; receives a new heart (Ezekiel 18:31)Behaviour adjusts temporarily; the heart remains unchanged
Direction of movementRuns to God for forgiveness (Luke 15:20)Runs from God in shame or hides (Genesis 3:8)
Biblical exampleDavid (Ps 51); Prodigal Son (Luke 15); Peter (Luke 22:62)Saul (1 Sam 15); Esau (Heb 12:17); Judas (Matt 27:3–5)

Case Study — False Repentance: Saul

1 Samuel 15:24–25

"I have sinned… because I feared the people and obeyed their voice. Now therefore, please pardon my sin and return with me."

⚠ Saul confesses but immediately shifts blame ("I feared the people"), focuses on reputation ("return with me"), and never genuinely turns. God rejected him as king.

Case Study — False Repentance: Judas

Matthew 27:3–5

"I have sinned by betraying innocent blood"… he went and hanged himself.

⚠ Metameleia — emotional remorse, not metanoia. Judas felt deep regret but ran away from God, not toward Him. Remorse without repentance leads to death, not life.

Case Study — True Repentance: The Prodigal Son (Luke 15:11–24)

  • 1
    "He came to himself" — Intellectual awakening; he sees his condition clearly (v.17)
  • 2
    "I will arise and go to my father" — Decision of the will; repentance is directional (v.18)
  • 3
    "I have sinned against heaven and before you" — Confession directed at God first (v.18)
  • 4
    "I am no longer worthy to be called your son" — True humility; no bargaining (v.19)
  • 5
    "He arose and came to his father" — He acted; repentance is not merely internal (v.20)
✓ Why the Distinction Matters

False repentance leaves a person in the same spiritual condition, only more emotionally exhausted. The test is not the intensity of emotion but the direction of movement — toward God or away from Him. A person can weep daily and never truly repent if the grief is self-directed rather than God-directed.

V

God Makes the First Move: Love as Initiation

God does not wait for us to come to our senses before acting. He initiates, He pursues, He provides — before we turn, so that we can turn.

◈ The Theological Claim

Repentance is not the human precondition that earns God's attention. It is the human response to God's prior action of grace. God's love is not reactive — it is proactive. He makes the first move in every act of restoration in all of Scripture.

Seven Biblical Patterns of God's Initiative

  • 1
    He seeks the lost before they seek Him
    Luke 15:3–7 — The Lost Sheep

    "What man… having a hundred sheep, if he has lost one… does not go after the lost one until he finds it?"

    God is the shepherd who initiates the search. The sheep does not find its way home — it is found.

  • 2
    He runs toward the returning sinner
    Luke 15:20

    "While he was still a long way off, his father saw him and felt compassion, and ran and embraced him and kissed him."

    The father was watching. He ran — undignified for a patriarch — because love could not wait. Restoration was initiated before the son's prepared speech was delivered.

  • 3
    He sends His Son before we ask
    Romans 5:8

    "While we were still sinners, Christ died for us."

    "While we were still sinners" — not after repentance, not after reformation. The cross preceded any human turning.

  • 4
    He pursues the rebellious with patient kindness
    Romans 2:4

    "God's kindness is meant to lead you to repentance."

    Every blessing, every mercy, every moment of life is a form of divine wooing — an invitation to turn.

  • 5
    He grants the very repentance He requires
    Acts 5:31 · Acts 11:18 · 2 Timothy 2:25

    "God exalted [Christ]… to give repentance to Israel and forgiveness of sins."

    He does not merely command repentance from afar — He supplies the capacity for it through His Spirit. This is the apex of grace: God requires what He Himself provides.

  • 6
    He initiated restoration in the Garden before the first repentance
    Genesis 3:9; Genesis 3:21

    "But the LORD God called to the man, 'Where are you?'"… "The LORD God made for Adam and his wife garments of skins and clothed them."

    Adam and Eve hid. God sought them. Before any recorded repentance, God asked the question that opened the door and provided covering — foreshadowing the cross.

  • 7
    He keeps calling — even when rejected
    Hosea 11:1–4, 8–9

    "When Israel was a child, I loved him… I took them up by their arms… How can I give you up, O Ephraim?… My compassion grows warm and tender."

    God describes Himself as a grieving parent who has been rejected — and yet cannot stop reaching out.

Malachi 3:7 — "Return to Me and I Will Return to You"

"Return to me, and I will return to you, says the LORD of hosts. But you say, 'How shall we return?'"

✓ The command to return is itself a gracious initiative. A silent God is a God of judgment only. A God who calls is a God who wants reconciliation — and He even answers the question of how.

◈ The Pattern Across the Whole Bible

In the Garden, God called first. In the Exodus, God heard Israel's groaning first. In the prophets, God sent messengers first. In the Incarnation, God became flesh first. On the cross, God acted first. At Pentecost, God poured out His Spirit first. In every single act of human repentance in Scripture, God has already moved. Repentance is not the door we open to let God in — it is our grateful response to a door He has already flung open.

VI

Why Repentance Is Critically Important

Without it, no salvation

Luke 13:3 — "Unless you repent, you will all likewise perish." It is the entry point to the kingdom.

Universal command of God

Acts 17:30 — God "commands all people everywhere to repent." No exceptions.

Brings forgiveness & new life

Acts 3:19 — "Repent and turn back, that your sins may be blotted out, that times of refreshing may come."

Ongoing, not once-for-all

Revelation 2–3 — Christ repeatedly calls churches to repent. It is a posture of the whole Christian life.

Restores relationship with God

Isaiah 59:2 — Sin separates. Repentance removes the barrier and restores fellowship.

Brings joy in heaven

Luke 15:7, 10 — "There is joy before the angels of God over one sinner who repents."

Inseparable from faith

Mark 1:15 — "Repent and believe." Repentance and faith are two sides of the same coin.

Protects the community

1 Corinthians 5:6–7; James 5:16 — Unconfessed sin spreads like leaven; repentance preserves the church.

"The LORD is near to the brokenhearted and saves the crushed in spirit." — Psalm 34:18

"Create in me a clean heart, O God, and renew a right spirit within me." — Psalm 51:10

All Scripture quotations from the English Standard Version (ESV). This study draws from both Testaments in their canonical unity.
