⚠ Read this first Advisory Preamble

Before you
type a word.

A plain-English note on privacy, data, and the rights you're quietly agreeing to trade.

01
The reality

Whatever you paste, somebody can see it.

Every AI service you use has access to everything you type into it. Depending on the service and the plan, that data may be used to train future models, shared with third-party partners, viewed by employees for "quality review," stored indefinitely — or all of the above.

Not every use is evil. But knowing which uses apply to you is your job, not theirs. The default terms rarely favor you.

02
Terms change

Read the agreement. Then remember they can rewrite it.

Meta didn't start out as the company it is today. Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp have rewritten their terms of service many times — each update quietly expanding what the platforms can do with what you post. Data once shared in confidence has ended up powering ad targeting, AI training, and products that didn't exist when you signed up.

The same applies to every AI service you're considering. What's private this year may not be private next year. A product that "doesn't train on your data" today may announce a change in a future email you'll scroll past. Grandfathering of old accounts is the exception, not the rule.

03
Paste rules

Rules of thumb for the paste box.

✕ Never paste

Customer SSNs or payment info · employee records · patient health info (PHI) · proprietary formulas or trade secrets · anything covered by a signed NDA · bank account numbers · passwords

⚠ Be careful with

Client names + detailed context together · competitor pricing intel · HR disputes · internal financials · draft legal language · unannounced product plans

✓ Generally safe

Drafting marketing copy · brainstorming ideas · summarizing public documents · learning concepts · reformatting text · editing your own writing

04
The one clause

The one setting that matters most.

Does this service use your inputs to train future versions of its models?

This is the question that separates "I used a tool" from "my work is now somewhere in the next model's training data." Services have different defaults. Some settings are buried three menus deep. Some can only be turned off on paid plans.

Before you paste anything proprietary, find the answer for your plan on your service. Not last year's answer. Not your friend's answer. Your plan, your account, today.

◆ When default isn't enough

If you're regulated or sensitive, consumer-grade AI isn't it.

Law, healthcare, finance, government — anything under HIPAA, SOC 2, FERPA, GLBA, or a specific data-protection requirement — the free version of any mainstream AI is probably the wrong tool. So is a $20/month consumer subscription whose terms you didn't read line-by-line.

A private or custom deployment through MM-Intelligence keeps your data on terms you actually control — in enterprise channels with explicit privacy protections, or in your own infrastructure — while still giving you the same power. If you have specific questions about what a service is doing with your data, or you want to ensure you aren't quietly giving away rights, that's exactly the conversation to have before you start pasting.

§ 01 — The Framework

Most bad AI output
isn't the AI's fault.

It's a one-line prompt asking for a five-paragraph result. The fix is a simple habit: before you hit Enter, walk through five ingredients.

C
Context

What's the situation? Who are you? Who is this for?

R
Role

Who should Claude be? Copywriter, CFO, lawyer, friend?

A
Ask

What exactly do you need? Be specific.

F
Format

Email, list, table, 200 words? Shape it.

T
Tone

Warm, professional, punchy, Southern-friendly?

✕ A weak prompt

"Write a marketing email for my bakery."

Generic output. Robotic. No voice. You'll throw it away.
✓ A CRAFT prompt

I own Sweetwater Bakery in downtown Montgomery. We specialize in from-scratch Southern desserts and weekday lunch plates. Act as a friendly copywriter. Write a promotional email to our existing customer list announcing our catering menu for graduation season. Under 200 words, with a clear subject line and one CTA button. Tone: a note from a neighbor, not a chain restaurant.

Rule of thumb

If your prompt fits in one line, it's probably too short. Aim for three to six sentences. If the first draft isn't quite right, don't start over — just say what to change.

§ 06 — The Automation Layer

Connect the dots while you sleep.

Claude is great at thinking. Zapier is great at doing — quietly moving information between the apps you already pay for so you stop being the human glue between your inbox, your spreadsheet, and your point of sale.

01
What it is

A "when this, then that" engine for your business.

Zapier links 7,000+ apps — Gmail, Google Sheets, Square, QuickBooks, Mailchimp, Calendly, Stripe, Shopify, HubSpot, Slack, and almost anything else with a login — and lets you build little automations called Zaps. Each Zap has a trigger ("when a new customer pays on Square…") and one or more actions ("…add them to my Mailchimp list, log the sale in Sheets, and text my bookkeeper").

You don't write code. You pick from menus. And as of 2026, Zapier has a built-in AI layer that lets Claude or ChatGPT sit inside your workflow — drafting the reply, classifying the lead, or summarizing the form before it ever hits your inbox.

Five places it earns its keep.

For Main Street businesses
01
Email & Inbox

Stop typing the same reply twice.

Auto-sort customer emails into folders by topic. Send personalized welcome notes when someone fills out your contact form. Get a daily Slack or text summary of every "high priority" email so you can stop refreshing Gmail.

Real example

"When a new email hits info@ with the word 'quote' or 'estimate' — Claude drafts a reply in my voice, attaches my pricing PDF, and parks it in my Drafts folder for one-click send."

02
Sales & Leads

Never lose a lead to the void again.

Capture every web form, DM, and missed call into one CRM. Notify yourself the second a hot lead lands. Schedule follow-up texts at 1 day, 3 days, and 7 days — automatically — until the prospect either books or replies.

Real example

"New Calendly booking → adds the contact to HubSpot, sends them a confirmation text, blocks my calendar, and puts a 'prep this lead' card on my Trello board with their LinkedIn pulled in."

03
Marketing

Cross-post once. Show up everywhere.

Post to Instagram and have it auto-publish to Facebook, LinkedIn, and a Google Business update. Sync new newsletter subscribers across Mailchimp, Klaviyo, and your customer list. Auto-tag returning customers as VIPs after their third purchase.

04

Forward a vendor invoice to a single email — Zapier extracts the amount, vendor, and date, drops it in QuickBooks, and saves the PDF to a "Bills" folder in Drive. Stripe payments auto-reconcile. Mileage tracker dumps into your tax spreadsheet weekly.

Real example

"New invoice in Gmail → AI reads the PDF, fills out the QuickBooks expense, files the receipt in Drive under the right folder by month. My CPA gets a clean handoff at quarter-end instead of a shoebox."

05

Answer the easy stuff at 2am.

Common "what are your hours" / "do you do X" / "where are you located" questions get an AI-drafted reply in seconds — pulled from a doc you wrote once. Claude only escalates the messages a human actually needs to handle.

The honest tradeoff.

Pros · Cons · Bottom line
✓ The Pros
  • Massive time savings. Most owners we work with reclaim 4–8 hours a week within the first month. That's a part-time hire's worth of output, paid back at $20–50/month.
  • No code required. Drag, drop, connect. If you can use a smartphone, you can build a Zap. Zapier's AI builder will even write the workflow for you if you describe it in plain English.
  • Works with what you already have. Square, QuickBooks, Gmail, Mailchimp, Shopify, Stripe — almost every tool a small business uses already has a Zapier connection.
  • Free tier is real. 100 tasks/month free. Most one-person businesses can validate the value before paying a dime. Paid plans start around $20/month.
  • Plays great with Claude & GPT. Drop an AI step into any workflow — write replies in your voice, classify leads, summarize long forms, translate messages, extract data from PDFs.
  • Audit trail built in. Every Zap logs every run. When something doesn't fire — or fires twice — you can see exactly what happened and fix it.
✕ The Cons
  • Costs add up fast. Once you have a few busy Zaps and want premium apps (most CRMs, QuickBooks, etc.), you're easily at $50–100/month. Multi-step Zaps consume more "tasks."
  • It will silently fail. An app updates its API, a password expires, a folder gets renamed — your Zap stops working and nobody notices for a week. You need a routine to check on them.
  • Garbage in, garbage out — at scale. A bad Zap doesn't make one mistake. It makes the same mistake 400 times before you find it. Test thoroughly. Run on fake data first.
  • Privacy is your problem. Customer data flowing through Zapier touches their servers and the AI services you've connected. See the Privacy Advisory — same rules apply. Don't auto-pipe SSNs, PHI, or payment info.
  • Vendor lock-in is real. After you've built 30 workflows, switching to Make, n8n, or anything else means rebuilding them. Pick deliberately.
  • It can hide problems. If your customer experience is broken, automation makes it broken faster. Fix the process before you automate it — or you'll just scale the mess.
◆ Where to start

Pick one annoying task.

The mistake people make with Zapier is trying to automate everything in week one. Don't. Pick the single task that wastes the most of your time this week — the one you'd happily pay $20 to never do again — and build that one Zap. Then live with it for a few days.

  1. a. Free account. Sign up at zapier.com. No credit card required.
  2. b. Connect 2 apps you use daily. Probably Gmail and Google Sheets, or your point of sale and Mailchimp.
  3. c. Build one Zap. Use Zapier's AI builder — type "When I get a new email about X, do Y." It will draft the workflow.
  4. d. Run it on fake data 3 times. Watch the logs. Make sure each step does what you expected.
  5. e. Turn it on. Check it in a week. If it saved you real time, build a second one. Compound from there.
◆ Bottom line

Zapier is the unglamorous superpower.

It will not do your strategy. It will not write your story. But for the boring, repetitive, copy-this-into-that work that quietly eats your week — it is the single highest-leverage tool a small business owner can adopt in 2026. Pair it with Claude and you get a Main Street business that runs like a much bigger one, without hiring like one.

§ 08 — Refrigerator Magnet

Pin this next
to your desk.

✓ Do
  • Give Claude a role. "Act as a copywriter." "Act as a CFO reviewing this."
  • Show, don't tell. Paste examples of what "good" looks like.

The people who get the most from AI are the ones who use it within 48 hours of learning about it. Here's the 50-minute plan to make sure that's you.

01
~20 min

This weekend

  1. a. Go to claude.ai and create an account.
  2. b. Create a Project for your business. Paste the instructions from the Claude Tools section.
  3. c. Upload three things: your About page, a past email you were proud of, your price list.
02
~30 min

Monday morning

  1. a. First 10 min — pick your most time-consuming weekly writing task. Run a CRAFT prompt.
  2. b. Next 10 — take a real customer email from last week. Paste into the reply drafter prompt.
  3. c. Final 10 — generate one week of social posts. Schedule them.
03
Ongoing

Over the next month

  1. a. Every time you write from scratch — stop. Ask Claude for a first draft.
  2. b. Keep a note of prompts that worked. That's your playbook.
  3. c. Teach one person on your team. Compounding starts when it's not just you.

You already know your business. Claude just helps you get it out of your head, onto the page, and out the door — faster.
