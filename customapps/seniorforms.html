📝 Filling Out Online Forms

Forms on the computer can feel tricky. Follow these steps and you will be fine.

Step-by-step guide:

  1. 1

    Read the whole form first before typing anything. Scroll down so you know what is coming.

  2. 2

    Gather your papers first. You may need your ID, Medicare card, Social Security number, medication list, or bank info.

  3. 3

    A red star (*) means required. You cannot skip those boxes.

  4. 4

    Click inside the box to type. The cursor (blinking line) shows where words will appear.

  5. 5

    Use "Tab" on the keyboard to jump to the next box, or just click on it.

  6. 6

    Double-check before you press Submit. Once sent, you usually cannot change it.

  7. 7

    Save or print the confirmation page after submitting. This is your proof.

⚠️ Safety warning:

Only fill out forms on websites that start with https:// (the "s" means secure) and that you trust. Never give your Social Security number on a form someone emailed or texted you out of the blue.

📞 Automated Phone Calls & Menus

When you call a company and a robot answers, do not hang up. These tips help.

✅ What to do:

  • Have a pen and paper ready
  • Listen to all the options before pressing
  • Say or press "0" to often reach a person
  • Say the word "agent" or "representative"
  • Stay on the line — do not hang up

❌ What to avoid:

  • Never give your Social Security number to someone who called you
  • Never give your bank or credit card info unless you called them
  • Do not press buttons you didn't hear clearly — press * or wait to repeat
  • Hang up on anyone pressuring you to act "right now"

Magic words that often get you to a human:

"Representative" "Agent" "Operator" "Speak to a person" "Customer service" Press 0

✉️ Handling Emails Safely

Scammers often use email to trick seniors. Here is how to stay safe.

🚨 Big warning signs of a scam email:

  • Says "urgent" or "your account will be closed"
  • Asks for your password, SSN, or bank info
  • Has strange spelling or odd grammar
  • The sender's email looks weird (like amaz0n-support@mail.ru)
  • Has a link or attachment you were not expecting
  • Offers a prize, lottery, or inheritance

Safe habits for email:

  1. 1

    Do not click links in an email unless you are 100% sure who sent it.

  2. 2

    If it claims to be from your bank, close the email. Call the number on the back of your card instead.

  3. 3

    Mark suspicious emails as "Spam" or "Junk" — don't just delete them.

  4. 4

    When in doubt, ask a trusted family member before responding.

🛠️ Services: Food & Transportation

🍲 Food Help

  • Meals on Wheels — Delivers hot meals to your home. Find a local program →
  • SNAP (Food Stamps) — Helps pay for groceries. Learn more (USDA) →
  • Senior Farmers' Market Program — Free coupons for fresh food. Check eligibility →
  • Local food pantries — Call 2-1-1 from any phone.

🚗 Transportation Help

  • Eldercare Locator — Finds rides in your area. Visit website →
  • Call 1-800-677-1116 — Free Eldercare phone line.
  • Local senior centers — Often have free rides to doctors and shopping.
  • Dial 2-1-1 — Tells you about local bus, van, or volunteer driver programs.

❤️ Health & Medical Help

📋 Medicare Help

  • Medicare.gov — Official website
  • Call 1-800-MEDICARE (1-800-633-4227)
  • SHIP — Free personal counseling

💊 Prescription Help

  • Extra Help Program
  • NeedyMeds.org
  • Ask your pharmacist about generic versions

🆘 In an emergency:

Call 911 if you have chest pain, trouble breathing, signs of a stroke (face drooping, arm weakness, slurred speech), or a bad fall.

Call 988 if you are feeling hopeless or having thoughts of hurting yourself. This is the free Suicide & Crisis Lifeline.

Questions to Ask About a Form

  • 🔹 "Is there a paper version of this form I can fill out instead?"
  • 🔹 "Can someone help me fill this out in person or on the phone?"
  • 🔹 "What happens after I submit this?"
  • 🔹 "When should I expect to hear back?"
  • 🔹 "What documents do I need to have ready?"
  • 🔹 "Can I save it and come back later?"
  • 🔹 "How will you contact me — by mail, phone, or email?"
  • 🔹 "Is there a fee for this?"

Questions to Ask on a Call

Write these down before you call:

  • 🔹 "May I have your name and ID number, please?"
  • 🔹 "Can you spell that?"
  • 🔹 "What is the reference number for this call?"
  • 🔹 "Can you repeat that more slowly?"
  • 🔹 "Can you send me that in writing?"
  • 🔹 "What is the next step?"
  • 🔹 "If I have more questions, who do I call?"
  • 🔹 "Is there a direct number or extension?"

Remember: A real company will wait while you take notes. A scammer will rush you.

Before Trusting an Email, Ask:

  • 🔹 "Was I expecting this email?"
  • 🔹 "Do I actually have an account with this company?"
  • 🔹 "Does the sender's email address look real?"
  • 🔹 "Is it pressuring me to act immediately?"
  • 🔹 "Is it asking for money, gift cards, or my password?"
  • 🔹 "Can I call the company directly (using a number I trust) to verify?"
  • 🔹 "Has a family member seen this email yet?"

Golden rule: If anything feels "off," it probably is. It's okay to ignore the email.

Questions to Ask Food Programs

  • 🔹 "Do I qualify? What are the income limits?"
  • 🔹 "Is this service free or is there a cost?"
  • 🔹 "What days and times do you deliver?"
  • 🔹 "Can you help with special diets (diabetic, low-salt, allergies)?"
  • 🔹 "What do I need to sign up? (ID, proof of income, address)"
  • 🔹 "Can my caregiver or family member sign up for me?"
  • 🔹 "How long does it take to start receiving meals?"
  • 🔹 "What do I do if nobody is home at delivery time?"

Questions to Ask Transportation Services

  • 🔹 "How far in advance do I need to book a ride?"
  • 🔹 "Is the ride free or is there a small fee?"
  • 🔹 "Can the driver help me in and out of the car?"
  • 🔹 "Is the vehicle wheelchair or walker accessible?"
  • 🔹 "Will the driver wait for me at the doctor?"
  • 🔹 "Can a family member or caregiver ride along?"
  • 🔹 "What is your service area?"
  • 🔹 "What happens if I need to cancel?"

Questions to Ask Your Doctor or Health Plan

For your doctor:

  • 🔹 "What is this medicine for, and what are the side effects?"
  • 🔹 "Can you write down the instructions for me?"
  • 🔹 "Is there a cheaper, generic version?"
  • 🔹 "When should I come back for a follow-up?"
  • 🔹 "What symptoms mean I should call you or go to the ER?"

For your health plan:

  • 🔹 "Is this doctor / hospital in my network?"
  • 🔹 "Is this service covered? How much will I pay?"
  • 🔹 "Do I need a referral or prior authorization?"
  • 🔹 "Can I get this in writing?"
