Part One

The Chapters.

Six acts, read in order. Each card expands. Click anything that looks clickable.

You will not get rich renting out your hours. You will get rich by building something that keeps working when you stop. A business, a product, a brand, a piece of software, a body of work, a network — an asset. The richest people in the world do not have unusual salaries; they have unusual leverage. The simplest way to think about wealth is this: specific knowledge, applied with judgment, compounded by leverage, over a long time.

The Equation
Wealth = (Value × Reach) − Costs, held over time.

You can move every variable. Most people only try to lower costs. Builders push value and reach upward, relentlessly.

Three Capitals
  • Financial — cash you can deploy.
  • Human — what you know and can do.
  • Social — who trusts and vouches for you.

A young founder is usually rich in two, poor in one.

The Four Forms of Leverage

Labor, capital, code, and media. The first two need permission — you must convince people to give you employees or money. The last two are permissionless — anyone can write software, anyone can publish. Modern wealth bends overwhelmingly toward the permissionless forms.

Founder's Checklist · Mindset

"Play long-term games with long-term people. All returns in life — wealth, relationships, knowledge — come from compound interest."

A business exists when someone pays you to solve a problem. Everything before that — the logo, the website, the name, the business cards — is procrastination dressed as progress. Before you build anything, write down in one sentence: Who has what pain that they're already paying to try to solve? If you cannot fill in the blanks, you don't have a business yet. You have a vibe.

The Problem Worth Solving Test

  1. Frequent — does it happen often enough to matter?
  2. Painful — does it cost them money, time, or status?
  3. Urgent — do they want it fixed now?
  4. Underserved — are current solutions bad, missing, or hated?
  5. Willing-to-pay — has money already moved toward solving this?

If you answer three of five, you have a candidate. Five of five is a gold mine.

Validation in the Cheapest Possible Way

Don't build. Sell. The most expensive validation is a finished product no one wants. The cheapest is a conversation. Aim for ten discovery conversations in your first week, conducted with the Mom Test method — ask about their past behavior, not their predicted behavior.

Cheap
Landing page + waitlist

A headline, a promise, an email field. Watch conversion.

Cheaper
Concierge MVP

Deliver the service by hand before automating anything.

Cheapest
Pre-sell it

Ask for money up front. Money is the only honest signal.

Script · The First Customer Call

"Walk me through the last time you tried to solve [problem]. What did you try? What did it cost you — in time, money, frustration? What would a perfect answer look like?"

Note: Never ask "Would you buy this?" You'll get lies. Ask what they've already done — you'll get truth.

"If you are not embarrassed by the first version of your product, you've launched too late."

Founders almost always start as the best doer in the room — and most collapse there. The job changes. At one person, you are the worker. At five, you are the coach. At thirty, you are the architect. At a hundred, you are the editor of decisions other people make. If you do not deliberately shed the last role as you take on the next, the company stops growing exactly at your personal throughput.

The Three Questions of a Leader

I.
Where are we going?

Vision. A sentence everyone can repeat without looking it up.

II.
How will we win?

Strategy. The non-obvious bets that make this possible.

III.
Who does what?

Clarity of roles. A single owner for every outcome.

Hire Slowly. Fire Quickly.

The most expensive mistake a small company makes is a bad hire who stays. Use the three-year test: would this person still be a top performer when the company is three times harder? If uncertain, don't hire. If certain they aren't, part ways with dignity and speed — your best people are watching.

The Operating Rhythm
Daily
Unblock. Remove one obstacle for each direct report.
Weekly
Metrics review. What moved, what didn't, why.
Monthly
Hiring, 1:1s, customer visits. Always.
Quarterly
Re-examine strategy. Kill one thing. Start one thing.

Culture Is What You Tolerate

Every value statement is fiction until tested. The real culture is whatever behavior you allow to go uncorrected. If you say you value honesty but don't confront a lie, you value comfort more than honesty, and everyone knows it. Leadership is mostly caring personally and challenging directly — at the same time.

"A players hire A players. B players hire C players. Your first ten hires determine the ceiling of everything that comes after."

Marketing, at its root, is the art of getting remembered by the right person at the right moment. In the beginning you cannot out-spend anyone, so you must out-care and out-specific them. Speak to ten real humans this week. Know their names. Write an email they'll actually open. Make the product embarrassingly personal. Then — only then — think about funnels.

The Positioning Statement

Fill this in until it stops being embarrassing:

For [specific kind of person] who struggles with [specific pain], our product is a [category] that [the one thing it does better], unlike [the default alternative].

Five Channels to Test (Pick One First)

Cold outreach

Personalized. Specific ask. High labor, high signal.

Content

Teach what you'd charge for. Let search find you.

Community

Go where they already gather. Help before you sell.

Partnerships

Borrow audiences. Offer real value in trade.

Paid ads

Last, not first. Scale a winning message — not a test.

The First-Ten Playbook
  1. List 100 ideal customers by name.
  2. Write 100 one-to-one messages. Personal, short, a single clear ask.
  3. Offer the first 10 an unfair deal — a discount, a call, a hand-delivered result.
  4. Treat each one like your only customer. Over-deliver visibly.
  5. Ask for a testimonial and two referrals. Always both.

The Three Metrics That Matter Early

CAC
Cost to acquire

Everything you spent, divided by new customers.

LTV
Lifetime value

Total gross profit a customer brings you, forever.

Payback
Months to even

How quickly does a customer pay you back your CAC?

A healthy rough target: LTV ≥ 3× CAC, payback under 12 months. Early on, just watch the trend.

"It's better to have 100 people who love you than 1,000,000 who merely like you."

Ambition without arithmetic is theater. If you want to build a business that does ten million in revenue, work backwards: what does that mean per month, per customer, per sales call, per hour of your attention? Suddenly the fantasy becomes a schedule. The job of a goal is to force that schedule into existence.

OKRs, Done Simply

Each quarter, pick one Objective — a qualitative direction — and three Key Results — measurable outcomes that, if achieved, mean you nailed the objective. If a key result doesn't have a number and a deadline, it's a wish. Measure What Matters covers this in depth.

Example · A Seed-Stage SaaS
Objective: Prove we are a must-have for mid-sized law firms.
  • KR1: 20 paying firms by end of Q2.
  • KR2: Weekly active usage ≥ 4 days/user.
  • KR3: Net revenue retention ≥ 110%.

Growth Is a Loop, Not a Line

Linear growth burns you out. Durable growth is a self-reinforcing loop: customers create content, content attracts customers; usage creates data, data improves product, better product drives usage. Sketch your loop on paper. If you cannot draw one, you are not growing — you are refilling a leaky bucket.

Founder's Drill · Reverse the Math

Write your annual revenue goal. Divide by average customer value. That's customers needed. Divide by your close rate. That's conversations needed. Divide by your channel response rate. That's the number of humans you must reach this year. Now: is your calendar set up to make that happen?

"You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems."

At zero revenue, your problem is that nobody knows you. At a hundred thousand, your problem is that you cannot ship fast enough. At a million, your problem is that you are the bottleneck. At ten million, your problem is culture. The problems never stop; they only change clothes. The founder's edge is emotional endurance — the ability to stay clear-headed, week after week, when nothing is clear yet.

The Five Stages & Their Main Danger

1
Nothing

Danger: giving up before talking to 30 customers.

2
First revenue

Danger: mistaking one-time buys for product-market fit.

3
Traction

Danger: scaling marketing before fixing retention.

4
Growth

Danger: your org chart is last year's, not next quarter's.

5
Scale

Danger: losing the edge that made you fun to work with.

Cashflow: The Only Thing That Kills You

Businesses don't die from bad ideas. They die from running out of cash. Know three numbers at all times: your monthly burn, your current cash, your months of runway. If runway drops below six months, cut costs today, not next quarter. If it drops below three, your only job is revenue or a raise.

Maintenance Drills — Every Week, Always
Customer
One customer call, personally. No exceptions.
Financial
Check cash, burn, pipeline. Three numbers.
Team
Skip-level with one person you don't know well.
Self
Sleep, movement, one long walk alone.

When Things Break

Every founder I respect has a version of this protocol: name the problem honestly; isolate it; ask what the best operator you know would do in the next 24 hours; do that; tell your team the truth. Silence and optimism are the two most expensive management habits. You can be direct and kind — that is the whole job.

"The only reliable way to build wealth is to keep showing up after it stops feeling new."
